Accurate assessment of cerebral hemodynamics impairment traditionally relies on 34 

arterial metrics, yet often overlooks venous drainage and arteriovenous dynamics, 35 

thereby limiting the evaluation of ischemia-induced microvascular dysfunction. To 36 

address this limitation, we implemented a signal-averaging framework, combined 37 

with co-fluctuation analysis, to extract predominantly arterial and venous 38 

hemodynamic signals and construct a dynamic arteriovenous co-fluctuation index that 39 

quantifies frame-by-frame coordination between arterial inflow and venous outflow 40 

activity. This time-resolved index enables spatial characterization of large-scale 41 

cortical arteriovenous coordination beyond conventional static correlation-based 42 

analyses. Comparative analyses between healthy controls and acute ischemic stroke 43 

mice demonstrated that the arteriovenous co-fluctuation index sensitively detects 44 

disruption of vascular coordination, revealing a slower state transition that occurs 45 

alongside distinct temporal abnormalities and regional heterogeneity between 46 

ischemic core and penumbral regions. These findings underscore the utility of 47 

arteriovenous coordination as a sensitive indicator of microcirculatory dysfunction, 48 

offering a practical analytical tool for assessing stroke-induced microvascular 49 

impairment. 50

## Introduction 55 

Recanalization therapies such as intravenous thrombolysis and endovascular 56 

mechanical thrombectomy are effective treatments for patients with large vessel 57 

occlusions (Jia et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2026; Sperring et al., 2023). However, a 58 

substantial proportion of patients continue to exhibit persistent neurological 59 

impairment despite successful macrovascular recanalization, a phenomenon 60 

commonly referred to as “no-reflow” (Jia et al., 2024; Sperring et al., 2023). This 61 

clinical dissociation indicates that restoration of large-vessel patency alone is 62 

insufficient to fully recover downstream microvascular perfusion and tissue viability. 63 

Preservation of ischemic penumbral tissue depends on coordinated function across the 64 

arterial-capillary-venous axis, requiring not only adequate arterial inflow but also 65 

effective venous drainage and the maintenance of capillary-level perfusion dynamics. 66 

Current clinical perfusion imaging approaches, including CT perfusion, MR 67 

perfusion, Doppler ultrasound, and positron emission tomography, primarily 68 

emphasize arterial perfusion-related parameters such as cerebral blood flow (CBF) 69 

(Kropf et al., 2025; Leigh et al., 2018), cerebral blood volume (CBV) (Lakhani et al., 70 

2025; Picchi et al., 2025), and mean transit time (MTT) (Bulut et al., 2022; Hilkens et 71 

al., 2024; Leigh et al., 2018). Although these metrics have substantially advanced 72 

stroke diagnosis and therapeutic decision-making, they remain predominantly focused 73 

on arterial supply and provide limited characterization of venous drainage dynamics 74 

and integrated arteriovenous coordination. Consequently, conventional perfusion 75 

metrics may incompletely capture tissue-level vascular dysfunction, particularly in 76 

regions where impaired venous outflow or disrupted vascular synchronization 77 

contributes to ongoing hypoperfusion despite preserved arterial inflow (Li et al., 2023; 78 

Proner et al., 2026). 79 

Ischemic stroke initiates a multiscale cascade of hemodynamic disturbances that 80 

progressively disrupt vascular coordination across both macrovascular and 81 

microvascular systems. At the macrovascular level, collateral circulation is rapidly 82 

recruited through redistribution of blood flow via pial anastomoses and 83

functional integration efficiency within whole-brain networks of the ischemic 85 

hemisphere (Niu, Wu, et al., 2024), suggesting that large-scale vascular compensation 86 

may be accompanied by adaptive network-level reorganization. At the microvascular 87 

level, tissue perfusion becomes impaired through mechanisms including capillary 88 

constriction, pericyte dysfunction, and increased vascular resistance (Sun et al., 2021; 89 

Xu et al., 2025). Importantly, our prior studies further demonstrated disruption of the 90 

normal temporal synchrony between arterial inflow and venous outflow dynamics 91 

following ischemia (Niu, Sihai, et al., 2024), representing a fundamental breakdown 92 

in arteriovenous coordination that extends beyond simple flow reduction. These 93 

observations collectively demonstrate that the functional outcome of microcirculation 94 

in ischemia is determined by integrated dysfunction across the 95 

arterial-capillary-venous axis, wherein impairment at any level compromises the 96 

viability of the entire microvascular unit. 97 

Despite increasing recognition of vascular coordination in cerebrovascular 98 

physiology, current in vivo optical imaging approaches remain limited in their ability 99 

to characterize dynamic interactions between arterial and venous hemodynamics. 100 

Although high-resolution imaging techniques can quantify parameters such as 101 

capillary red blood cell velocity, perfused capillary density, and tissue blood volume 102 

(Gong et al., 2025; B. X. Liu et al., 2025; Wang et al., 2021; Xu et al., 2025), these 103 

approaches typically generate composite hemodynamic signals arising from 104 

sequential vascular compartments, including arteries, capillaries, and veins. This 105 

spatial overlap makes selective isolation of arterial and venous hemodynamic 106 

signatures particularly challenging in large-scale cortical imaging datasets. 107 

Several technical factors contribute to this limitation. First, the dense spatial 108 

interdigitation of cortical arteries and veins creates substantial anatomical overlap, 109 

complicating compartment-specific signal segregation (Park et al., 2023; Schmid et al., 110 

2019). Second, because optical imaging methods detect hemodynamic activity 111 

through erythrocyte-derived contrast signals, both arterial and venous compartments 112 

contribute strong signal intensity, particularly in regions where vessels are closely 113

from vessels located at different cortical depths (Schmid et al., 2019). As a result, 118 

superficial veins and deeper penetrating arterioles may contribute overlapping signals 119 

that are difficult to reliably separate using conventional post-hoc. 120 

Consequently, venous drainage dynamics and coordinated arteriovenous 121 

interactions remain underrepresented in conventional cerebrovascular analyses. To 122 

address this limitation, we developed a co-fluctuation-based analytical framework to 123 

characterize the dynamic coordination between arterial and venous blood flow activity. 124 

Inspired by the theoretical framework of Esfahlani et al. (Zamani Esfahlani et al., 125 

2020), demonstrating that static functional connectivity can be decomposed into 126 

frame-wise co-fluctuation events, we reformulated negative arteriovenous correlations 127 

as temporally evolving anti-phase fluctuation dynamics. By computing instantaneous 128 

products of normalized arterial and venous signals across time, we generated 129 

continuous arteriovenous co-fluctuation time series that transform conventional static 130 

correlation measures into a dynamic index of vascular coordination. This framework 131 

enables time-resolved assessment of arterial-venous synchronization and provides a 132 

systems-level approach for investigating vascular dysfunction in ischemic stroke 133 

beyond traditional artery-centric perfusion models. 134

Resting-state CBF imaging was performed in each animal within 72 hours 190

2.4 Signal Preprocessing Pipeline 194 

Resting-state CBF imaging was preprocessed using a standardized pipeline 195 

developed and validated in our research (Niu et al., 2026; Niu, Wu, et al., 2024). The 196 

preprocessing workflow began with spatial normalization: cortical spatial masks were 197 

generated based on stereotaxic coordinates from rat and mouse brain atlases, and 198 

affine registration was applied to align individual CBF maps to a standard rat brain 199 

template. Individualized masks were then applied to each CBF image to exclude 200 

non-cerebral background and environmental noise. 201

The CBF time series was preprocessed via four sequential steps: (1) Denoising: 202 

Raw RGB images were converted to gray images and smoothed twice with Gaussian 203 

filters. The first pass used a 5 × 5 pixel kernel with a standard deviation of 1.3 to 204 

suppress mechanical noise (White et al., 2011), the second employed a Gaussian 205 

kernel with a full width at half maximum of 5 pixels to enhance the signal-to-noise 206 

ratio through spatial smoothing (Bergonzi et al., 2015). (2) Head motion regression: 207 

Head motion parameter was extracted from the background activity and regressed out 208 

from the blood flow signal to minimize motion-related artifacts. (3) Outlier 209 

correction: Temporal outliers in the time series were corrected using nearest-neighbor 210 

interpolation. (4) Detrend: Linear trends and very slow oscillations (< 0.01 Hz) were 211 

removed from the time series to eliminate slow signal drift. 212 

2.5 Vascular Mask Construction Framework 213 

A critical limitation of optical imaging is its limited depth of field (L. J. Liu et al., 214 

2025; Park et al., 2023), which prevents clear discrimination between arteries and 215 

veins at different cortical depths ( Fig. 1A ). To address this constraint, we developed a 216 

comprehensive segmentation framework to construct reliable arterial and venous 217 

masks, integrating multiple strategies across spatial scales and anatomical regions to 218 

ensure methodological robustness, anatomical accuracy, and broad applicability. 219 

Strategy 1: high-resolution manual segmentation. 220 

In the rats' somatosensory-motor cortex (n = 19), which is densely vascularized, 221 

five distinct compartments were manually segmented based on morphological criteria: 222 

large arteries, small arteries, brain parenchyma, veins, and mixed arteriovenous 223 

regions ( Figs. 1B, S1 ). This labor-intensive approach generates anatomically faithful 224 

reference masks with precise boundary definition, serving as a benchmark for 225 

automated segmentation validation. However, the extensive manual effort limits its 226 

wide application across entire brain volumes. 227 

Strategy 2: representative ROI placement. 228 

Multiple ROIs in the healthy mice (n = 30) were selected from arterial trunks, 229 

venous trunks, and non-vascular brain parenchyma (Figs. 1C, 4A ), serving as 230 

representative samples of arterial, venous, and background signals, respectively. This 231

2.6 Validation of Signal Purification Robustness 253 

To verify the reliability of our spatial averaging strategy for arterial and venous 254 

signal purification, we conducted a comprehensive robustness assessment through 255 

three complementary analyses: 256 

(1) Signal homogeneity within vascular masks. 257 

Using manually segmented arterial, venous, and mixed vascular masks, we 258 

computed functional connectivity matrices from all pixels within each mask. 259 

Distribution characteristics of correlation coefficients, including kurtosis and 260 

skewness, were analyzed to quantify signal heterogeneity. Distributions exhibiting 261 

high kurtosis and positive skewness reflected synchronized blood flow activity and 262 

minimal compositional heterogeneity, whereas deviations from this pattern suggested 263 

potential high heterogeneity. In addition, the proportion of negative correlations 264 

within each matrix was quantified to measure cross-contamination between arterial 265 

and venous compartments. 266 

(2) Stability of the spatial averaging procedure. 267 

Bootstrap resampling was performed on individual vascular masks at 268 

progressively increasing sampling densities (ranging from 5% to 95% of the total 269 

number of pixels) to generate random subsets of the original signal population. For 270 

each subset, the averaged time series was compared against the full-mask reference 271 

using Pearson correlation and phase difference. High similarity at low sampling rates 272 

demonstrated the stability of the spatial averaging approach. 273 

(3) Quantifying resilience to segmentation errors. 274 

To assess robustness against realistic segmentation errors, we synthetically 275 

mixed arterial mask signals with increasing proportions of venous signals (0.05 to 276 

0.50 in 0.05 steps), generating a continuum of hybrid signal profiles. The correlation 277 

between each hybrid signal and the original arterial signal was tracked across mixing 278

2.7 Methodological Framework for Dynamic Arteriovenous Coupling Analysis 282 

Recent advances by Esfahlani and colleagues (Zamani Esfahlani et al., 2020) 283 

established a mathematical insight showing that static functional connectivity emerges 284 

from the temporal integration of instantaneous co-fluctuation patterns across brain 285 

networks. This insight has prompted a paradigm shift in neuroscience, moving the 286 

field from static to dynamic analyses of brain connectivity. The co-fluctuation 287 

approach quantifies the instantaneous synchronization of hemodynamic signals 288 

between brain regions via edge-time series analysis. Its temporal average is 289 

mathematically equivalent to the Pearson correlation, thereby providing a rigorous 290 

mathematical foundation for reinterpreting static connectivity within a dynamic 291 

framework. 292 

Our prior investigation revealed that arterial and venous blood flow signals in the 293 

cerebral cortex exhibit anti-phase oscillations and negative correlations (Niu, Sihai, et 294 

al., 2024). This observation challenges traditional interpretations of arteriovenous 295 

relationships, suggesting that negative correlations reflect not merely static statistical 296 

properties, but rather the cumulative outcome of discrete, transient anti-phase 297 

fluctuation events occurring throughout the hemodynamic time series. Guided by this 298 

co-fluctuation perspective, we developed a novel framework for constructing 299 

arteriovenous co-fluctuation time series, transforming the Pearson negative 300 

correlation into a frame-by-frame arteriovenous circulation index. This approach 301 

enables direct quantification of coupling dynamics between arterial inflow and venous 302 

outflow, allowing real-time tracking of microvascular coordination. The methodology 303 

comprises three integrated components, as shown in Figure 2: 304 

Step 1: Vascular blood signal purification. We generated mean time series for 305 

the arterial and venous compartments by spatially averaging blood flow signals across 306 

all voxels within their respective vascular masks. This spatial averaging effectively 307

2.8 Characterizing Spatiotemporal Dynamics of Microcirculatory Activity 339 

The co-fluctuation analysis framework was used to dynamically characterize 340 

cerebral blood flow microcirculatory states through integrated temporal and spatial 341 

pattern analysis, aiming to elucidate microcirculatory functional impairments induced 342 

by stroke. 343

Given that temporal co-fluctuation amplitudes exhibited a right-skewed 344 

distribution, prompting us to define the top 5% of timepoints based on amplitude 345 

distribution as high-coupling states, and the bottom 5% as low-coupling states. The 346 

former represents optimal microcirculatory coordination, and the latter reflecting 347 

poorest coordination. Principal component analysis was applied to timepoints 348 

corresponding to these two states, with spatial activity patterns derived from the first 349 

principal component to compare regional blood flow distribution between high and 350 

low coupling conditions. To further evaluate stroke-induced alterations in 351 

microcirculatory dynamic transitions, arteriovenous time series from both stroke and 352 

control groups were normalized and combined for k-means clustering (cluster number 353 

ranging from 3 to 6) to identify shared functional states. State-specific metrics, 354 

including temporal occupancy, duration, occurrence times, self-transition probability, 355 

and between-state transition probabilities, were employed to compare groups and 356 

quantitatively characterize stroke-related abnormalities in microcirculatory state 357 

transitions. 358 

To reveal spatial patterns of CBF and microcirculatory impairment, we used the 359 

contralateral hemisphere as an internal reference to quantify ischemic injury severity. 360 

For each region in the ischemic hemisphere, we calculated the difference in signal 361 

intensity relative to its contralateral homolog as a measure of regional damage. Based 362 

on established criteria from previous studies (Biose et al., 2020; C. Li et al., 2022; Li 363 

et al., 2013), regions below 70% of the contralateral value were classified as the 364 

ischemic core, and those with 50–70% of the contralateral intensity were classified as 365 

the ischemic penumbra. Intra-regional homogeneity was assessed using Pearson’s 366 

correlation to quantify regional coherence, complemented by phase difference 367 

analysis to evaluate regional synchronization. Finally, we examined associations 368 

between cortical vascular composition and ischemic vulnerability indicators including 369 

ischemic volume and penumbra/core ratio. Correlation analyses were performed, with 370 

significance assessed via FDR-corrected P-values (q < 0.01) and model fitting 371 

evaluated using correlation coefficients. 372

3.1 Characterization of Cerebral Blood Flow in Masks of Different Vasculatures 392 

In vivo optical imaging of cerebral hemodynamics faces a fundamental 393 

resolution constraint: the limited optical depth-of-field precludes clear spatial 394 

separation of closely positioned arteries and veins at different cortical depths (Glück 395 

et al., 2024; Park et al., 2023). When optical blur and light scatter cause signal overlap 396 

between vessels at distinct depths, mathematical isolation of pure arterial or venous 397 

components becomes technically infeasible, even with post-hoc signal processing ( Fig. 398 

1A ). Current research usually overlooks this limitation (Fischer et al., 2023; Mo et al., 399 

2023; Wang et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2021), either treating mixed vascular signals as 400 

homogeneous or focusing exclusively on arterial dynamics, thereby hindering a 401 

penetrating understanding of arteriovenous coordination mechanisms. We therefore 402

3.2 Blood Flow Signal Purification and Noise Effects on the Purification Process 464 

In the previous section, we demonstrated significant spatial heterogeneity in 465 

arteriovenous coupling across cortical regions. In high-coupling regions, tightly 466 

interwoven arterial and venous networks hinder signal separation. In low-coupling 467 

regions, vascular signals separate more clearly with better internal consistency within 468 

masks. However, these observations merely characterize structural-functional 469 

relationships and do not yield practical methods for the complete separation and 470 

purification of arterial and venous signals. This methodological gap limits both the 471 

accurate assessment of venous drainage and a deeper understanding of arteriovenous 472 

coordination mechanisms. To address this limitation, we implemented a manual 473 

ROI-based approach to define five distinct vascular compartments ( Fig. 4A ): arteries, 474 

veins, brain tissue, and their intersection regions. By quantifying how non-target 475 

vascular signals interfere with signal purification within each mask, we established a 476 

generalizable framework for arteriovenous signal separation and purification. 477 

To isolate pure blood flow signals, we implemented a signal averaging technique 478 

and evaluated its performance through two complementary approaches. First, we 479 

examined signal convergence characteristics by progressively expanding ROI sizes 480 

and quantifying the technique's efficacy in extracting pure vascular signals from 481 

mixed inputs. Second, we assessed noise resilience by measuring interference caused 482 

by both homogeneous vascular noise (originating from the same vessel type) and 483 

heterogeneous vascular noise (from different vessel types), thereby establishing the 484 

method's performance boundaries under distinct noise conditions. 485 

First, we manually selected ROIs to quantify blood flow signals from five 486 

distinct vascular compartments in the cerebral cortex ( Fig. 4A ): arteries, brain tissue, 487 

veins, artery-tissue junctions, and vein-tissue junctions. For each ROI, we performed 488 

signal averaging on randomly selected pixel subsets of varying sizes, using the mean 489 

signal from all pixels serving as the reference standard. Our results demonstrated that 490

3.3 Spatiotemporal Decoding of Whole-Brain Arteriovenous Microcirculation 543 

Cerebral blood flow delivers essential oxygen and metabolic substrates to 544

for understanding brain physiology. However, optical imaging faces a persistent 546 

challenge: arterial and venous signals on the cortical surface cannot be fully separated 547 

(Glück et al., 2024; Park et al., 2023), severely compromising measurements of 548 

microcirculation. In our previous work, we mitigated this limitation by extracting 549 

venous signals and applying regression analysis to suppress their contamination (Niu 550 

et al., 2026; Niu, Wu, et al., 2024). Although this approach enhanced signal purity, 551 

residual venous influence persisted. Our early analysis revealed a key physiological 552 

insight: during resting states, arterial and venous blood flow signals exhibit robust 553 

anti-phase oscillations with strong negative temporal correlation (Niu, Sihai, et al., 554 

2024). This intrinsic vascular coordination offers a natural basis for signal separation. 555

transforms the arterial-venous negative correlation coefficient into a frame-by-frame 557 

vascular circulation index. Multiplying this index by the original CBF signal 558 

effectively isolates microcirculatory activity, enabling precise characterization of 559 

cortical hemodynamics. 560

## Discussion 700 

In this study, we applied a co-fluctuation analysis framework to transform the 701 

inverse temporal relationship between arterial and venous hemodynamic activity into 702 

a frame-by-frame arteriovenous circulation index for assessing cerebral 703 

microcirculatory dynamics. Methodological validation demonstrated that both arterial 704 

and venous vascular masks contain heterogeneous signal components; however, 705 

spatial signal averaging effectively suppressed this contamination and enabled robust 706 

extraction of predominantly arterial and venous hemodynamic time series. This 707 

approach permitted characterization of large-scale arteriovenous coordination and 708 

construction of an arteriovenous circulation coefficient reflecting dynamic vascular 709 

synchronization. Under physiological conditions, cortical microcirculatory activity 710 

exhibited a conserved spatial organization, with strong activity observed in 711 

sensorimotor cortices and comparatively lower activity in temporal and limbic regions. 712 

In the ischemic stroke model, regions exhibiting stronger arteriovenous coordination 713 

demonstrated relative resistance to ischemic injury, whereas regions with weaker 714 

coupling suffered more severe impairment. By comparison, the ischemic penumbra 715 

maintained a more preserved microcirculatory and perfusion function than the infarct 716 

core, which was severely impaired. Temporal analyses further revealed that ischemic 717 

stroke disrupted normal microcirculatory dynamics, characterized by prolonged 718 

persistence within individual functional states and reduced inter-state transition 719 

probability. 720 

4.1 Physiological Basis of Arteriovenous Oscillatory Coupling 721 

The cerebral circulation functions as an integrated physiological network that 722 

coordinates arterial inflow, capillary-level exchange, and venous drainage to maintain 723

Furthermore, phase analysis (Glück et al., 2024) revealed that healthy cerebral 743 

vasculature maintains an arterial-venous phase difference exceeding 120°, which 744 

diminishes to approximately 60° during acute ischemic episodes. Collectively, these 745 

observations establish that the anti-phase relationship between arterial and venous 746 

flow is a fundamental characteristic of normal cerebral perfusion and a sensitive 747 

biomarker of impaired vascular coordination after ischemic injury. Importantly, while 748

4.2 Methodological Validation for Separating Arterial and Venous Signals 756 

To ensure accurate separation of arterial and venous blood flow signals, we 757 

conducted a comprehensive validation of our signal separation methodology. In 758 

optical imaging, the spatial interweaving of vascular networks frequently results in 759 

individual pixels containing mixed arterial and venous components. Direct analysis of 760 

raw signals, therefore, introduces substantial confounding effects arising from 761 

overlapping flow dynamics, potentially compromising subsequent arteriovenous 762 

coupling analyses. We therefore rigorously evaluated our separation framework to 763 

determine whether the extracted arterial and venous signals reliably reflected their 764 

dominant hemodynamic profiles, thereby establishing a methodological basis for 765 

subsequent analyses of arteriovenous coordination. 766 

To assess the feasibility of arterial-venous signal separation, we manually 767 

segmented cortical vascular masks. Because the sensorimotor cortex exhibits 768 

particularly high vascular density, five vascular compartments were defined: 769 

artery-dominant, vein-dominant, arteriolar, parenchymal tissue, and arteriovenous 770 

mixed regions. Quantitative analysis based on signal decomposition demonstrated that 771 

compartment-specific signal contribution was below 85% in both venous and 772 

arteriovenous mixed regions, whereas arteriolar and parenchymal tissue regions 773 

exhibited substantially higher signal specificity. These findings indicate intrinsic 774 

limitations in completely separating arterial and venous signals within highly 775 

interwoven vascular territories. The close spatial proximity of cortical vascular 776 

structures, together with their tight metabolic and hemodynamic coupling, constrains 777 

signal isolation (Grandjean et al., 2023; Xiong et al., 2017). This observation is 778 

consistent with previous neurovascular imaging studies demonstrating that highly 779 

interconnected vascular regions present substantial challenges for 780 

compartment-specific signal extraction (Park et al., 2023; Schmid et al., 2019). 781 

Based on these findings, we developed an optimized framework combining ROI 782 

placement and semi-automated vascular segmentation to improve arterial-venous 783

The validated signal purification approach provides a methodological foundation 811 

for reframing the study of cerebral microcirculation. Rather than conceptualizing 812 

cerebral blood flow as a static transport system, our findings support the interpretation 813

Within this framework, arteriovenous synchronization may represent an emergent 815 

systems-level property reflecting coordinated vascular regulation across the 816

For decades, research on blood flow dynamics has centered on static vascular 818 

metrics (Boas & Dunn, 2010; Jin et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2025), such as cerebral blood 819

evidence (Gutierrez-Barragan et al., 2022; Klugah-Brown et al., 2026; Lee et al., 2024; 827 

Yang et al., 2023) challenges the assumption of fixed temporal organization within 828 

brain networks. Instead, real-time coordination within brain networks exhibits 829 

aperiodic, spontaneous rhythmic characteristics (L. Li et al., 2022; Zamani Esfahlani 830 

et al., 2020). These time-varying characteristics unlock unique neural fingerprints that 831 

are specific to individual subjects (Lee et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2023; Zamani 832 

Esfahlani et al., 2020), offering a promising pathway toward personalized 833 

neurofunctional profiling. 834 

In addition, our results provide compelling evidence for the fundamental 835 

principle that cerebral vascular architecture is exquisitely tailored to functional 836 

demands. The spatial organization of arteriovenous networks demonstrates 837 

remarkable regional specialization: in high-metabolic regions such as the 838 

sensorimotor cortex, dense and tightly coordinated arterial-venous patterning 839 

facilitates superior hemodynamic performance, whereas territories with relatively 840 

sparse venous networks demonstrate reduced circulatory efficiency and weaker 841 

arteriovenous synchrony. This vascular patterning mirrors evolutionary adaptations in 842 

rodents, whose sensorimotor systems are critical for whisker-based environmental 843

and have evolved optimized vascular networks to meet their high-energy demands. 845 

Collectively, these observations provide compelling hemodynamic evidence for the 846 

fundamental principle of structure-function adaptation. 847 

In summary, our analytical framework represents a conceptual advance in 848 

cerebrovascular research. Rather than focusing on isolated vascular parameters or 849 

static perfusion metrics, our approach captures the dynamic coordination between 850 

arterial supply and venous drainage across the entire vascular network. This paradigm 851 

shift not only overcomes the limitations of traditional artery-centric analyses but also 852

4.4 Evaluating Microcirculatory Dysfunction Induced by Ischemic Stroke 855 

Ischemic stroke has long been considered a disorder of arterial insufficiency, 856 

with therapeutic paradigms centered on vascular recanalization and restoration of 857 

cerebral blood flow (Hilkens et al., 2024; Kropf et al., 2025). However, nearly half of 858 

patients still experience persistent hypoperfusion, motor dysfunction, and incomplete 859 

cognitive recovery despite successful reperfusion therapy, challenging the sufficiency 860 

of arterial-centric pathophysiological models. Recent investigations increasingly 861 

implicate venous outflow obstruction as a critical contributor to stroke pathogenesis 862 

(Li et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023). The interplay between arterial insufficiency and 863 

venous congestion provides a possible framework for explaining the clinical paradox 864 

of successful reperfusion but poor functional outcomes. Based on this integrative 865 

perspective, we propose that stroke-induced ischemia involves microcirculatory 866 

failure, mediated by dysfunction of the arteriovenous circulation. To validate this 867 

hypothesis, we developed a novel assessment framework quantifying arteriovenous 868 

fluctuation coupling and implemented it in an acute ischemic stroke model to 869 

characterize microcirculatory dynamics. 870 

Our findings demonstrate that stroke-induced microcirculatory impairment 871 

manifests across both temporal and spatial dimensions. Temporally, post-stroke 872 

arteriovenous dynamics exhibited delayed state transitions, consistent with previous 873

reports describing cerebrovascular sluggishness and impaired vascular responsiveness 874 

following ischemic injury (Østergaard et al., 2016; Wang et al., 2023). Spatial 875 

analyses revealed marked regional heterogeneity in ischemic vulnerability: visual 876 

cortical regions characterized by relatively sparse arteriovenous integration exhibited 877 

more severe disruption of circulation dynamics, whereas somatosensory regions with 878 

denser mixed vascular architecture exhibited comparatively preserved function. 879 

Although these findings do not establish causality, they suggest that regions with 880 

stronger arteriovenous integration may possess greater hemodynamic resilience, 881 

potentially through enhanced collateral compensation or improved redistribution of 882 

blood flow following ischemia (Glück et al., 2024; Xiong et al., 2017; Xue et al., 883 

2025). Quantitative analyses further demonstrated that artery-dominant vascular 884 

composition correlated positively with ischemic lesion extent, whereas higher 885 

proportions of mixed arteriovenous regions correlated inversely with lesion severity 886 

and positively with the penumbra/core ratio. These findings should be interpreted 887 

cautiously, as they likely reflect region-specific vascular organization rather than 888 

direct causal protection. 889 

Of note, the microcirculation-based method yielded ischemic lesion maps highly 890 

consistent with those from the conventional CBF approach, confirming its comparable 891 

performance in identifying ischemic lesions. Beyond standard CBF, our 892 

microcirculation framework further characterizes ischemic injury by integrating 893 

temporal dynamic patterns and spatial heterogeneity, thereby providing 894 

multidimensional diagnostic insights unavailable from conventional perfusion 895 

analysis. Furthermore, our approach achieves a more pronounced separation between 896 

the ischemic penumbra and infarct core, facilitating a more rigorous assessment of 897 

ischemic heterogeneity. Collectively, the proposed strategy not only preserves the 898 

lesion-detection capability of standard CBF approaches but also expands the 899 

analytical dimensions for evaluating stroke-induced injury, offering a novel 900 

perspective for the comprehensive diagnosis of acute stroke. 901

Several limitations should be acknowledged. First, the present framework is 903 

derived primarily from cortical surface laser speckle imaging and therefore does not 904 

directly capture deep-brain or capillary-level microcirculatory dynamics. Accordingly, 905 

the term “microcirculation” in this study should be interpreted as large-scale cortical 906 

microvascular coordination rather than direct visualization of capillary exchange. 907 

Second, the arteriovenous circulation index represents an indirect surrogate measure 908 

of vascular synchronization rather than a direct quantification of perfusion efficiency 909 

or oxygen delivery. Third, because all imaging experiments were performed under 910 

isoflurane anesthesia, anesthesia-related modulation of vascular oscillations and 911 

neurovascular coupling may influence the observed dynamics. Fourth, although signal 912 

purification analyses demonstrated strong robustness, definitive validation against 913 

ground-truth vascular labeling methods, such as fluorescent angiography or 914 

two-photon microscopy, was not performed. Finally, the observed associations 915 

between vascular architecture and ischemic vulnerability remain correlational and 916 

should not be interpreted as evidence of direct causal protection. 917 

4.6 Conclusion 918 

Using a co-fluctuation analysis framework, we developed a dynamic 919 

arteriovenous circulation index that quantifies the temporal coordination between 920 

arterial inflow and venous outflow during cerebral blood flow activity. Application of 921 

this framework in ischemic stroke mice demonstrated that disruption of arteriovenous 922 

synchronization is associated with altered microcirculatory dynamics and regional 923 

ischemic vulnerability. These findings provide a systems-level perspective on 924 

cerebrovascular dysfunction that extends beyond conventional artery-centric 925 

perfusion analyses and may offer a useful framework for future investigation of 926 

microvascular pathophysiology in ischemic stroke. 927
