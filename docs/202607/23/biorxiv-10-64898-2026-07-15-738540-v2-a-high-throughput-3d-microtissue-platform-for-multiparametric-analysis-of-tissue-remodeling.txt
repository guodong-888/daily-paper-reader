ABSTRACT 1

Extracellular matrix (ECM) remodeling and force generation are fundamental drivers of tissue 2

morphogenesis and repair, yet scalable methods to  quantitatively  interrogate these dynamic 3

mechanical processes remain limited. Here, we present a high-throughput screening platform that 4

integrates engineered three-dimensional (3D) microtissues within a standardized 96-well format. 5

We introduce a robust mold-casting fabrication process and a layer-by-layer surface modification 6

strategy, that ensures long-term tissue stability and prevents detachment (95% tissue formation 7

success; stable in culture for more than 10 days). This system enables simultaneous, longitudinal 8

quantification of tissue closure, tissue contractility, and tissue compaction from a phase-contrast 9

imaging modality. The computational data analysis tools that accompany this framework ensure 10 

reproducibility through deterministic computation and accelerate data extraction 80-fold relative 11 

to manual annotation. Using pharmacological compounds, we show that tissue closure dynamics, 12 

force generation, and compaction represent independent variables of ECM-driven tissue 13 

remodeling, challenging assumptions embedded in commonly used contraction-based assays. 14 

Furthermore, benchmarking against reported clinical drug responses demonstrates that the 3D 15 

platform better aligns with clinical outcomes (Kendall τ-b = 0.72, p= 0.045 , n =8/10) than a 16 

conventional two-dimensional scratch wound assay (Kendall τ-b = 0.52, p= 0.25 , n =4/10). 17 

Together, this work establishes a scalable assay for functional screening and quantitative 18 

assessment of tissue remodeling dynamics in three-dimensional systems. 19

I. INTRODUCTION 21 

Tissue morphogenesis emerges from the coordinated interplay between cellular contractile 22 

forces, extracellular matrix (ECM) assembly, and ECM remodeling, which together sculpt three-23 

dimensional tissue architecture in development, repair, and disease. 1–3 The ECM is not a passive 24 

scaffold, its biochemical composition and hierarchical organization regulate how cells generate 25 

force while cell-generated forces reciprocally reorganize matrix architecture through protein 26 

unfolding, matrix compaction and fiber realignment. 4–7 As a result, ECM remodeling operates 27 

across multiple hierarchical scales, from molecular turnover of matrix components to tissue-scale 28 

reorganization of matrix architecture and mechanics. 8–11 29 

30 

Although biochemical assays have provided important insight into pathways governing matrix 31 

synthesis and degradation, tissue morphogenesis and repair ultimately arise from functional 32 

remodeling processes, including tissue compaction, force generation, matrix organization, and 33 

collective tissue movements that emerge from cell-matrix interactions. 12,13 Quantifying these 34 

tissue-scale remodeling behaviors remain a major challenge in mechanobiology because existing 35 

experimental models rarely combine dynamic measurements of functional remodeling with 36 

experimental scalability. 14,15 37 

38 

A major obstacle is the persistent trade-off between physiological relevance and experimental 39 

scalability. In vivo models retain physiological context but they are low-throughput and poorly 40 

suited for controlled, multifactorial perturbation studies. 16 Conventional in vitro assays, 41 

including widely used planar scratch-wound assays 17 and traction-based force measurement 42

Here, we present a scalable 3D microtissue platform for the quantitative analysis of functional 49 

tissue remodeling. This system enables longitudinal measurements of tissue closure, contractile 50 

force generation, and tissue compaction, within ANSI-standard 96-well plate formats. Flexible 51 

micropillar force sensors are integrated with engineered microtissues anchored through an 52 

optimized surface chemistry, while WoundCompute, an open-source automated image analysis 53 

pipeline, automatically extracts multiparametric remodeling dynamics across large experimental 54 

cohorts without manual intervention. By integrating reusable 3D-printed fabrication modules 55 

with automated analysis, this framework enables quantitative dissection of functional tissue 56 

remodeling processes that have been difficult to interrogate at scale. 57 

58 

To demonstrate the scalability and analytical power of this framework, we performed 59 

multiparametric screening of compounds targeting cytoskeletal contractility, matrix remodeling, 60 

and wound repair pathways. This analysis revealed that tissue closure, contractile force 61 

generation, and tissue compaction are not intrinsically coupled outputs, as often implied by 62 

conventional contraction-based assays, but instead represent differentially regulated axes of 63 

functional tissue remodeling. Comparison with conventional 2D scratch assays further showed 64 

that 3D microtissue responses more closely align with clinically observed therapeutic behaviors. 65

II. RESULTS 69 

A 96-well microtissue platform for multiparametric analysis of tissue repair dynamics. 70 

Previous work from our group demonstrated that engineered stromal microtissues repair injury 71 

through coordinated tissue contraction, extracellular matrix assembly, and matrix compaction, 72 

identifying these processes as key drivers of fibrous tissue repair. 6,7,25–27 However, these studies 73 

were performed in low-throughput formats, which limited systematic perturbation and 74 

longitudinal, multiparametric analysis. To quantitatively dissect these remodeling processes at 75 

scale, we engineered a 96-well microtissue platform that integrates three-dimensional tissue 76 

assembly and repair with simultaneous mechanical and structural readouts (Fig. 1). 77 

78 

The platform combines a scalable hardware design with an integrated imaging and analysis 79 

workflow. Each well contains an array of four flexible polydimethylsiloxane (PDMS) 80 

micropillars that support microtissue formation and enable mechanical readout (Fig. 1a, b; 81 

Supplementary Fig. 1). To fabricate these devices reproducibly and at scale, we developed a 82 

reusable mold-casting system with 12 modular 3D-printed assemblies, each holding eight PDMS 83 

molds for casting individual micropillar devices directly into wells (Fig 1a, Supplementary Fig. 84 

1a, b). After seeding normal human dermal fibroblasts (NHDFs) in a collagen type I matrix, 85 

anisotropic microtissues suspended between opposing pillars self-assembled within 12–24 hours 86 

(Fig. 1a). 25,28 To initiate repair-like tissue remodeling, we introduced a controlled laser ablation 87

Platform reproducibility supports scalable microtissue screening. 119 

To support larger-scale experiments with reliable parallel measurements, we quantified well-to-120 

well variability and spatial artifacts across the 96-well platform (Fig. 2c-h). 35,36 Prior to injury, 121 

engineered microtissues exhibited highly uniform tissue footprint across plates, with a coefficient 122 

of variation (CV) of 7.57%, indicating consistent tissue formation (Supplementary Fig. 2c, d). 123 

After injury, the primary functional endpoint, time to 50% gap closure (T 50 ), showed moderate 124 

well-to-well variability (CV 17.58%) (Fig. 2c). Tissue footprint was highly uniform across plates 125 

(CV 3.74%) (Fig 2e), whereas the change in average pillar force showed modest variability (CV 126 

20.47%) (Fig 3-3e). Importantly, we detected no systematic positional biases or edge effects 127 

across the plate (Fig. 2d, f, h). Together, these findings establish the platform as a reproducible 128 

and scalable assay compatible with high throughput screening (HTS) workflows 35,36 , and show 129 

that tissue formation, closure dynamics, and contractile forces can be measured consistently 130 

across experimental replicates. 131

To enable analysis of large imaging datasets, we developed WoundCompute, an open-source 134 

Python-based analysis framework with graphical user interface (GUI) functionality for 135 

automated quantification of tissue repair dynamics. (see Appendix S1 for details; code available 136 

at GitHub repositories:  WoundCompute , and  WoundComputeGUI ; image data associated with 137 this study available at  10.6019/S-BIAD2719 ). Automation was necessary due to the scale of data 138 

collected; a single 48-hour experiment with 96 wells imaged every 30 minutes produced more 139 

than 9,000 images, rendering manual annotation impractical and susceptible to user bias and 140 

annotator fatigue (see Appendix S2 for details on our Published Microscopy Dataset). 141 

WoundCompute accepts raw phase contrast microscopy images and applies an automated 142 

segmentation pipeline to isolate the gap region, surrounding tissue, and micropillar cap positions. 143 

These segmentation masks enable quantitative extraction of closure kinetics, tissue compaction, 144 

and contractile force measurements. In addition to frame-by-frame analysis, the software 145 

performs longitudinal time-series analysis to identify complete closure events and detect 146 

structural collapse of microtissues (Fig. 3a, b). 147 

The analysis pipeline processes individual images in approximately 12 seconds and, through 148 

parallelization, achieves an 80-fold acceleration over manual annotation. This enables full 149 

analysis of HTS experiments within approximately 3 hours 30 mins on a workstation equipped 150 

with AMD Ryzen™ 9 5900X processor (12 core), and 64 GB of DDR4 RAM running Microsoft 151 

Windows 11 (see Appendix S1 for details). By applying a fixed segmentation criterion uniformly 152 

across all samples, WoundCompute eliminates user-dependent variability and ensures greater 153 

reproducibility relative to manual annotation. 154

Multiparametric phenotyping reveals independent variables of tissue remodeling. 169 

Timelapse analysis captured a biphasic remodeling response consisting of a transient gap 170 

expansion followed by progressive, complete repair within 24 hours (Fig. 1d). These dynamics 171 

were accompanied by coordinated changes in tissue compaction, as the projected tissue footprint 172 

decreased during early remodeling before stabilizing as closure progressed (Fig. 1e). In parallel, 173 

micropillar deflection measurement revealed dynamic modulation of contractile forces 174 

characterized by an initial loss of tension immediately post-injury, followed by force recovery 175 

during closure that transiently overshot pre-injury baseline levels (Fig. 1f). 176

III. DISCUSSION 227 

Tissue repair is coordinated by dynamic interactions between cells, ECM, and mechanical forces, 228 

yet most scalable assays reduce this process to a single endpoint: gap closure. This simplification 229 

obscures how tissues remodel, transmit force, compact matrix, and recover structural continuity. 230 

To address this gap, we developed a 96-well microtissue platform that combines micropillar-231 

based force measurement with tissue-scale imaging to quantify stromal remodeling in a format 232 

that is both mechanically informative and experimentally scalable. By integrating reusable 3D-233 

printed modules, standard phase-contrast microscopy, and an open-source analysis pipeline, the 234 

platform provides access to assessing tissue remodeling processes that are difficult to capture in 235 

conventional 2D assays and impractical to measure at scale in existing 3D systems. 49–51 236 

A key component of this workflow is WoundCompute, an open-source computational framework 237 

for automated analysis of microtissue remodeling across large experimental datasets. Existing 238 

image-analysis tools often require proprietary software, or have limited batch-processing 239 

automation, insufficient documentation, and inaccessible source code, creating barriers to 240 

reproducibility and adoption. 52–60 Moreover, supervised learning approaches introduce an 241 

additional constraint i.e. when manual annotations are treated as ground truth, model accuracy is 242 

inherently bounded by the consistency of human labeling. As shown in Appendix S1, identical 243 

instructions can yield substantial annotator-to-annotator variation, therefore limiting 244

Multiparametric phenotyping revealed that closure, compaction, and contractile forces are related 249 

but separable features of tissue repair. Hierarchical clustering of pharmacological perturbations 250 

identified distinct remodeling signatures associated with contractile motor activity, adhesive 251 

coupling, and proteolytic matrix remodeling. Inhibition of the non-muscle myosin II motor with 252 

blebbistatin, which blocks ATPase activity downstream of both the Rho-kinase (ROCK) and 253 

myosin light chain kinase (MLCK) pathways, simultaneously impaired closure, contractile force 254 

generation, and tissue compaction. 64,25,29,42 This phenotype is consistent with disruption of the 255 

common actomyosin machinery that drives both force generation and fibrillar matrix 256 

densification. Dasatinib produced a similar reduction in contractile tissue force, compaction and 257 

closure, but its interpretation is less straightforward. As a broad-spectrum tyrosine kinase 258 

inhibitor targeting Src-family kinases, Abl, PDGFR, 65,66 it also suppresses ECM production, 259 

including collagen synthesis 67 , making it difficult to attribute this phenotype to a single 260 

molecular target. 261 

In contrast, selective ROCK inhibition with Y-27632 impaired closure with reduced tissue 262 

contractile force but increased tissue compaction. This phenotype suggests that relieving global 263 

stress-fiber tension while preserving MLCK-dependent myosin activity allows local collagen 264 

densification and cell packing to continue despite diminished tissue-scale forces. Alternatively, 265 

increased compaction in the presence of impaired closure may reflect altered focal adhesion 266 

turnover and matrix remodeling that are not captured by measurement of contractile force 267

IV. METHODS 302 

Mold Fabrication and Assembly 303 

To achieve both the high-fidelity resolution required for microscale pillar force-sensor 304 

templating and the modularity necessary for scalable handling, we developed a hybrid 305 

fabrication strategy combining two-photon polymerization (2PP) and stereolithography (SLA) 306 

(Supplementary Fig. 1A). We utilized 2PP to fabricate a master mold containing inverse cavities 307 

for a four-pillar array (1 mm x 0.6 mm) situated within a 1.12 × 1.5 mm microwell 308 

(Supplementary Methods). Each pillar features a 150 μm diameter cylindrical base, a 200 μm 309 

diameter spherical cap, and a total height of 450 μm (Fig. 1B). To scale production without 310 

degrading the original master, we established a replica molding pipeline and fabricated a 4x5 311

Plate fabrication 322 

To mitigate edge effects driven by uneven evaporation, we utilized 96-well plates featuring a 323 

PBS-fillable perimeter moat (Nunc Edge 2.0 96-well plate, Thermo Scientific, Cat No. 324 

267578). 79 Within each well, PDMS devices were fabricated by vacuum-casting PDMS onto the 325 

assembled 8×12 mold array, inserting the molds into the plate, and curing at 60 °C for at least 6 326 

hours. 77,78 Once de-molded, the plate was sonicated with 70% ethanol in each well to clean the 327 

fabricated devices, then air dried and stored for later use. 328 

Surface treatment optimization 329

Microtissue formation and tissue repair model 348 

To generate 3D microtissues, cells were suspended in neutralized liquid rat tail type I collagen 349 

(2.2 mg/mL, Corning) and seeded into each device in 0.9 μL volumes. To prevent premature 350

per well for optimization and a standard density of 4,000 cells per well was established for all 353 

subsequent experiments. Following loading, devices were inverted and incubated at 25 °C for 15 354 

minutes, followed by 37 °C for 15 minutes to ensure uniform collagen polymerization. Finally, 355 

each well was supplemented with FGM2 containing 50 μg/mL L-ascorbic acid-2-phosphate, and 356 

cells were allowed to drive tissue assembly overnight at 37 °C. 357

WoundCompute processing, manual processing and measurements 367 

WoundCompute is software designed to quantify wound area, tissue footprint, and pillar 368 

positions automatically at every imaging timepoint; full details of the WoundCompute 369 

segmentation and tracking pipeline are provided in Appendix S1. Briefly, individual micropillars 370 

were segmented from phase contrast images using Otsu thresholding and morphological 371 

operations (i.e., closing, dilation, erosion, and opening), with pillar cross-sections identified via 372 

ellipse fitting and clustering. Pillar positions were tracked across frames using masked template 373 

matching with normalized cross-correlation. Wound and tissue segmentation were performed 374 

through a separate pipeline comprising Gabor filtering, median and Gaussian smoothing, and 375 

Otsu thresholding, where the combined pillar mask defined an admissible region within which 376 

the wound was identified as the largest connected component. The tissue mask was obtained by 377 

inverting the thresholded image and retaining the largest connected component not touching the 378 

image boundary. The pipeline is implemented as a modular, test-driven framework in which each 379 

processing step is independently validated through unit tests. Pillar tracking accuracy was 380 

validated by applying synthetic displacements of known magnitude, and the recovered synthetic 381 

displacements were assessed against the known ground truth. Segmentation was evaluated by 382 

comparing WoundCompute extracted wound and tissue area against manual annotations on a 383 

subset of images, where manual segmentation served as a reference check rather than a ground-384 

truth benchmark. WoundCompute runs on all three major operating systems (Microsoft 385 

Windows, MacOS, and Linux) and is openly available under the MIT License. 386

Scratch Wound Assay 409 

The scratch wound assay was performed as described in 17,81 . Briefly, human dermal fibroblasts 410 

(NHDF-Neo or NHDF-Ad) were seeded in a 96 well plate at a density of 20,000 cells/well in 411 

FGM2 media supplemented with 50 ug/mL L-ascorbic acid-2-phosphate. Cells were grown to 412 

95% confluency at 37°C and 5% CO 2 . A linear scratch was created in each monolayer using an 413 

Agilent BioTek AutoScratch device. Wells were washed twice with PBS to remove dislodged 414 

cells and fresh media containing either vehicle (0.1% DMSO) or one of the small molecules with 415 

reported clinical outcomes: Crenolanib (10 μM), Cisplatin (10 μM), Dasatinib (200 nM), 416 

Lenvatinib (10 μM), Metformin HCl (10 μM), Methotrexate (10 μM), Nintedanib(200 nM), 417 

PDGF-BB (10 ng/mL), Semaglutide (100 nM), Tirzepatide (100 nM). 418 

Images of the wound were captured immediately following injury and every 30 minutes 419 

thereafter for 48 hours using an Agilent BioTek Cytation 10 microscope (10X objective). The 420 

wound area was quantified at each time point using Gen5 Image Prime software (Agilent 421 

BioTek), and wound closure was normalized to the initial wound area. 422 

Statistics 423 

All standard statistical analyses were performed in Python 3 (SciPy and scikit-learn). Prior to 424 

analysis, data distribution normality was assessed using the Shapiro-Wilk test, and variance 425 

equality was confirmed via the F-test (for two groups) or the Brown-Forsythe test (for multiple 426 

groups). Outliers were identified using the Grubbs’ test (α = 0.05) and excluded before normality 427 

was re-evaluated. For comparisons between two independent groups, normally distributed data 428 

were analyzed using an unpaired, two-tailed Student’s t-test (equal variance) or Welch’s t-test 429 

(unequal variance). Non-normally distributed data were evaluated using the Mann-Whitney U430 

test. Statistical significance was defined as P < 0.05. 431
