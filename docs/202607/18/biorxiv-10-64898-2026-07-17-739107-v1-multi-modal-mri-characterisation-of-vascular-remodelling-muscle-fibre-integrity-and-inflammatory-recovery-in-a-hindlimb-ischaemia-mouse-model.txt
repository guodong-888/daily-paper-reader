# Abstract 36 The hindlimb ischaemia (HLI) mouse model is a widely used preclinical model of 37 chronic limb-threatening ischaemia (CLTI). While CLTI involves complex interactions 38 between impaired perfusion, inflammation and muscle wasting, the standard imaging 39 approach, laser Doppler imaging (LDI), only assesses perfusion. MRI is used 40 clinically to assess neural tracts, inflammation, and perfusion in the brain. We 41 therefore evaluated whether a multimodal MRI approach could longitudinally monitor 42 recovery in the HLI mouse model. Mice underwent MRI three days pre-HLI surgery, 43 and on Days +3 and +7 post-surgery, with histology on Day +7. The MRI detected 44 significant increases in muscle volume and inflammation after HLI surgery, with 45 significant decreases in perfusion, vascular length, and muscle fibre integrity. Overall, 46 MRI can monitor inflammation, muscle fibre integrity, and vascular recovery post-HLI 47 and should be applied in future studies to identify mechanisms of therapeutic 48 recovery in a sequential in vivo analysis without requiring animal sacrifice. 49 50

The 55 resulting reduction in blood flow creates a severely hypoxic and inflammatory tissue 56 environment, leading to progressive tissue damage and loss of limb function [1]. CLTI 57 presents as ischaemic rest pain, non-healing ulcers and/or gangrene in the affected 58 lower limb. An estimated 10-25% of CLTI patients do not respond to standard 59 revascularisation therapies and continue to progress to advanced disease, placing 60 them at risk of major limb amputation [2,3]. Overall, CLTI carries a poor prognosis, 61 with a 5-year mortality rate typically exceeding 50% [4,5]. Consequently, there is an 62 urgent need to develop novel therapeutic strategies for this patient population. 63 The hindlimb ischaemia (HLI) mouse model is widely used as a preclinical model for 64 evaluating potential therapeutics for CLTI, with laser Doppler imaging (LDI) being the 65 most common imaging modality to longitudinally monitor blood flow recovery following 66 HLI induction [6–9]. LDI provides high-resolution (0.1 mm) assessment of limb 67 perfusion recovery but does not quantify the inflammatory response, muscle 68 volumetric changes, or alterations in muscle architecture, all of which are substantially 69 affected in CLTI and HLI. This is particularly relevant as many therapeutics being 70 investigated for CLTI, including mesenchymal stromal cells, exert pleiotropic effects 71 that extend beyond revascularisation, including promotion of muscle regeneration and 72 the reduction of inflammation and fibrosis [10–12]. In most cases, endpoint histology 73 is required to quantify these effects. Longitudinal assessment of these parameters in 74 vivo would therefore require euthanasia and tissue collection at each timepoint, 75 preventing serial evaluation within the same animal. 76 Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) is routinely used clinically for the non-invasive 77 monitoring of pathophysiology in soft tissues. Advances in MRI technology have 78 enabled the assessment of pathophysiological changes in the HLI pre-clinical model; 79 however, several groups have primarily focused on the use of MRI to monitor 80 vascular recovery over time [13–17]. This study aims to integrate complementary MRI 81 sequences and analytical techniques in a longitudinal multimodal imaging framework 82 to investigate the HLI mouse model, enabling the simultaneous assessment of 83 vascular remodelling, muscle integrity, and inflammatory recovery over time [13,18]. 84 By combining these complementary analyses, MRI can characterise the temporal 85

Overall, this study demonstrates 328 the potential of MRI to longitudinally monitor recovery in the HLI mouse model, 329 capturing the muscular, inflammatory, and vascular responses through the combined 330 use of 3D-GRE, ASL, 3D-TOF, DW-EPI and FLAIR imaging. 331 Patients with CLTI exhibit substantial loss of muscle mass and function [29], a 332 phenotype that our lab and others have also observed in the HLI mouse model 333 [12,25]. This decline is likely attributable to impaired nutrient delivery, ischaemia-334 induced tissue necrosis, and reduced mechanical loading, which collectively promote 335 muscle atrophy and structural deterioration. Here, we have shown that MRI can be 336 used to track changes in muscle volume across time. At an early timepoint of Day +3 337 post-HLI we identified a significant increase in muscle mass using volumetric analysis 338 of MRI images (Figure 2). This increase in volume is likely due to the oedema 339 associated with the ischaemia induced inflammation, which was corroborated in the 340 FLAIR imaging [30]. We predict this muscle mass will decrease at later timepoints as 341 shown by Evans et al. [25], as suggested by the drop in muscle volume on Day +7. 342 While data by Scalabrin et al [31] noted a significant decrease in gastrocnemius and 343 soleus weights at Day 7 post-HLI, our data did not find significantly differences. The 344 difference is likely attributed to Scalabrin et al transecting both the femoral artery and 345 vein, causing a more severe phenotype. Sustained inflammation is a key hallmark of 346 limb ischaemia and is known to contribute negatively to tissue remodelling and 347 functional recovery. The hypoxic and inflammatory environment act as potent 348 chemoattractant stimuli, promoting rapid immune cell infiltration while contributing to 349 extensive tissue necrosis and oedema. Our histological data similarly shows 350 significant immune cell infiltration and tissue necrosis at Day +7 (Figure 3). MRI 351 analysis demonstrated water content changes, a surrogate marker of inflammation, 352 and demonstrated significant oedema on both Day +3 and +7. In addition, at Day +7 353 histological assessment revealed early muscle remodelling, characterised by mild 354 collagen deposition and large areas of muscle fibre degeneration, although extensive 355 collagen accumulation and adipocyte infiltration were not yet evident. The absence of 356 adipocyte accumulation suggests that this represents an early remodelling stage prior 357

It facilitates longitudinal analysis of multiple parameters in vivo without 386 the need for animal sacrifice. Nevertheless, LDI may remain a more practical 387 approach for longitudinal monitoring of perfusion recovery due to its simplicity and 388 resolution, and perhaps can be applied in parallel.
