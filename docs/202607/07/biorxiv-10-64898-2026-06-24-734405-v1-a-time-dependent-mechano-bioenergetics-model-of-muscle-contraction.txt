Abstract 7

Predictions of skeletal muscle energy consumption under a diverse range of muscle contractile conditions 8

are critical for improving our understanding of locomotion. Existing mathematical models, while capturing 9

the mechanical dependence of energy consuming processes, neglect the time-dependent behaviour and recov-10 

ery costs associated with regenerating ATP. This time-dependence is important for predicting the energetic 11 

response of muscles during repetitive or cyclical tasks like locomotion, where muscle undergoes many con-12 

traction cycles. This study presents a novel model to predict energetic rates based on physiological processes: 13 

Ca 2+ transport costs, cross-bridge cycling costs, and ATP regeneration. Previous mathematical models in-14 

clude the dependence on Ca 2+ transport and cross-bridge cycling, but neglect the time-dependent response 15 

and the subsequent recovery of ATP following the contraction. Model parameters were obtained from exist-16 

ing data on isolated muscle preparations, and predicted energetic rates were validated on separate datasets 17 

across a range of contractile conditions including dynamic, sub-maximal, and twitch contractions. The time-18 

dependent model was able to capture the influence of contraction frequency on peak energetic rates and the 19 

time-course of energetic recovery observed experimentally. The model captures key physiological processes 20 

while maintaining a minimal number of free parameters and low computational cost. This enables generalis-21 

ability across muscles and species, and implementation into larger scale musculoskeletal models. 22 23 

Keywords: Biomechanics, Calcium, Mathematical modelling, Metabolic cost, Muscle mechanics 24

Estimates of skeletal muscle energy consumption over the course of a contraction cycle are critical for understand-26 

ing a muscle’s function during any locomotor task and for predicting the energetic costs of movement. While it 27 

is possible to experimentally obtain direct measures of energy use in vivo (averaged over many seconds (Haeufle 28 

et al., 2020) or minutes of steady-state conditions (Cavagna et al., 1977)), isolated ex vivo muscle preparations 29 

are required to obtain the precise time-dependent energetic responses of muscle (Woledge et al., 1985). In many 30 

movements, such as walking or running, muscles undergo repeated cyclical contractions, meaning that aver-31 

aged values are not capable of giving us insight into the relationship between muscle mechanical state (muscle 32 

strain, strain rate, and activation level) and energy use; thus, mathematical models are required to estimate 33 

these relationships. However, existing models (e.g., Konno et al. (2025) and Lichtwark and Wilson (2005)) rely 34 

predominantly on the mechanical state, and neglect the muscle history and recovery processes that are also key 35 

determinants of muscle energy use (Barclay & Curtin, 2023; Lichtwark et al., 2025). 36 

Energy, in the form of ATP, is consumed during a muscle contraction to transport Ca 2+ into the sarcoplasmic 37 

reticulum and to cycle the actin-myosin cross-bridges. Both of these processes are dependent on the mechanical 38 

state of the muscle (Woledge et al., 1985), and as muscle shortens at faster rates it produces more heat (Fenn, 39 

1923). Existing energy models account for these contributors (Umberger et al., 2003), but critically, are not mod-40 

elled based on the true energy consuming processes. Instead, the activation heat associated with Ca 2+ transport is 41 

typically calculated as proportional to the level of activation and not to the rate of Ca 2+ transport (Konno et al., 42 

2025). Further, given that the rate of Ca 2+ transport depends on the concentration of Ca 2+ within the cytoplasm 43 

of muscle fibres, current models cannot account for the history dependence on the rate of Ca 2+ transport. 44 

As ATP is the fuel consumed during the muscle contraction, its supply needs to be regenerated through either 45 

aerobic or anaerobic processes. These regeneration mechanisms are not completely efficient, and heat is released 46 

during the conversion of the energy stored in substrates (i.e., glucose or palmitate) to ATP (Barclay, 2019). 47 

These energy costs are of similar magnitude to the initial costs of generating force (Barclay, 2019), and are thus 48 

likely an important contributor to total in vivo energy use, particularly during repeated contractions or longer 49 

durations. While existing models captured the time-course of ATP regeneration (Kushmerick, 1998), they have 50 

not been coupled with models that capture the initial consumption of ATP, nor the inefficiencies in this process, 51 

limiting their ability to capture whole muscle energy use. 52 

In this study, we present and validate a novel mechano-bioenergetics model accounting for the time-dependent 53 

processes governing the energetics of muscle contraction, including both the initial mechanisms capturing ATP 54 

consumption and the subsequent regeneration of ATP. The initial contractile energetics model is based on Konno 55 

et al. (2025), accounting for the mechanical dependence of muscle energetic rates, with modifications to directly 56 

model Ca 2+ transport costs. To capture the time-course of ATP recovery, we implemented a bioenergetics model 57 

based on Kushmerick (1998) with an additional component accounting for the inefficiencies of the regeneration 58 

process. The model parameters are informed using data from isolated mouse muscle preparations (Barclay et 59 

al., 2010; Barclay et al., 1995), and validated on separate datasets across a range of contractile conditions and 60 

stimulation intensities (Barclay, 2012; Barclay & Weber, 2004; Lewis & Barclay, 2014; Mast & Elzinga, 1987, 61 

1988). 62 

# 2 Methods 63 

## 2.1 Mathematical model 64 

The modelling framework consists of a combination of individual models capturing the process of muscle con-65 

traction (Fig. 1): i) the excitation-activation model captures the flow of Ca 2+ upon stimulus and the subsequent 66 

binding of Ca 2+ to troponin, ii) the mechanical Hill-type model simulates the muscle mechanical state (strain, 67 

strain rate, and force), iii) the initial energetics model captures the energetic cost associated with Ca 2+ pumping 68 

into the sarcoplasmic reticulum and cross-bridge cycling, and iv) the bioenergetics model captures the recovery 69 

processes including the ATP-PCr dynamics and the corresponding energetic rate. 70

Figure 1: Model workflow. The mathematical model takes as input a stimulus, either a motor unit firing or external stimulation. From the stimulus, the excitation-activation model computes the Ca 2+ concentrations and the activation state ( ∝ [CaT n ]). The activation state is used by the mechanical model to solve for the muscle strain, strain rate, and force. The outputs from these two models are then used by the initial energetics model to determine the energy used during the contraction, which gives the ATP consumed by ATPases. The initial energetics model computes the heat lost as inefficiencies to Ca 2+ transport (parameter rcat ), cross-bridge cycling (parameter rcxb ), and additional heat lost to inefficiencies during shortening (parameter rshort ). The regeneration of ATP consumed is determined using a bioenergetics ATP-PCr model, which solves for the ATP and PCr levels within the muscle. The rate of regeneration of ATP is calculated as a function of the ADP within the muscle. Here we assume oxidative recovery, and the heat released during regeneration of ATP is proportional (parameter rrec ) to the rate of regeneration of ATP. ## 2.2 Model Parameters 150 

The excitation-activation model parameters were chosen based on experimental data from isolated muscle ex-151 

periments (see Table 1). The parameters of the Hill-type model have been described previously (Dick et al., 152 

2017) with adjustments made for ˙ ϵce,max for mouse muscle fibres (Barclay et al., 2010; Kissane & Askew, 2024). 153 

The initial energetics parameters ( rcxb , rcat , rshort , and scxb ) were determined through an optimisation to the 154 

dataset from Barclay et al. (2010). The recovery energetic rate, rrec , and the bioenergetic recovery model pa-155 

rameters, nox and ϕox,max , were optimised to match the experimental time-course of recovery (Barclay et al., 156 

1995) and subsequently corrected to match the magnitude of the recovery rates with changes to temperature 157 

(Barclay, 2019; Barclay & Weber, 2004). A detailed description of the experimental protocols are within the 158 

Supplementary Material. 159 

Initial energetics parameters. The initial energetics model parameters were obtained based on data in the 160 

mouse soleus and extensor digitorum longus (EDL) from Barclay et al. (2010), consisting of energetic rates 161 

measured at shortening velocities from isometric to maximum strain rates (5.2 s−1 for soleus and 8.9 s−1 for the 162 

EDL). We assumed that the relative contributions from isometric heat production are split such that 60% is due 163 

to cross-bridge cycling and 40% is due to Ca 2+ transport (Ca 2+ transport contributions typically vary between 164 

25 to 45% (Barclay & Curtin, 2023)). The increase in energetic cost with faster shortening rates (Fenn, 1923) 165 

is given by the shortening heat component. To obtain scxb , we enforced that the ratio between cross-bridge 166 

cycling and Ca 2+ transport heat was maintained across stimulation frequencies (Lewis & Barclay, 2014). Thus, 167 

we minimised the objective function 168 

L = Lcxb + Lcat + Lshort + Lratio , (20) where Lcxb and Lcat enforce the cross-bridge and Ca 2+ transport heat components, respectively. Lshort enforces 169 

the mechanical dependence on the shortening rate. Lratio enforces the ratio in Ca 2+ transport heat to total initial 170 

heat across stimulation frequencies. Objective function details are contained within the Supplementary Material. 171 

Bioenergetics model parameters. The bioenergetics model parameters {rrec , n ox , ϕ ox,max } were optimised to 172 

the experimental data set from (Barclay et al., 1995), where muscle underwent repeated cyclical contractions 173 

with a contraction and rest phase. The experimentally determined initial heat rates were used as input to the 174 

bioenergetics model, which makes this model independent of the parameters determined in the initial energetics 175 

model. The experimental force traces for the EDL demonstrated fatigue, potentially leading to greater ineffi-176 

ciencies in the recovery process not representative of aerobic recovery; thus, we only optimised the parameters to 177 

the soleus dataset and applied these parameters to the EDL. The parameters were obtained through minimising 178 

the difference between the experimental recovery heat per cycle, ˙qrec,cycle,exp , and the recovery heat per cycle 179 

predicted by the model, ˙ qrec,cycle,model ,180 

Lrec = || ˙qrec,cycle,model − ˙qrec,cycle,exp || 2. (21) 

## 2.3 Validation 181 

To validate the model predictions under independent contractile conditions to those in which we developed our 182 

model (Barclay et al., 2010; Barclay & Weber, 2004), we compared model outputs to experimental data from 183 

across a range of contractile conditions and stimulation frequencies (twitch to tetanus) from Barclay and Weber 184 

(2004), Barclay (2012), Lewis and Barclay (2014), Mast and Elzinga (1987), and Mast and Elzinga (1988). To 185 

assess both the initial and bioenergetic models, we compared to experimental data for repeated dynamic contrac-186 

tions where the time-course of recovery was measured (Barclay & Weber, 2004). These conditions consisted of 187 

repeated contractions at contraction frequencies from 0.5 to 4 Hz with an overlapping stimulation and shortening 188 

phase with the shortening rate chosen to optimise muscle work. Another validation was performed to ensure the 189 

time-course captured the energetics when measured using oxygen measures (rather than heat measures). This 190 

was done by comparing the time-course of recovery with the experimentally measured time-course following con-191 

secutive twitches (Mast & Elzinga, 1987). To assess model performance during sub-maximal contractions (more 192

# 4 Discussion 233 

With parameters informed and validated on separate datasets, we found that the mechano-bioenergetics model 234 

predicted a similar time-course and magnitude of energetic rates to those observed experimentally (Barclay, 235 

2012; Barclay & Weber, 2004; Lewis & Barclay, 2014; Mast & Elzinga, 1987, 1988). Further, it captured the 236 

time-dependence and recovery process not included in existing models. While the model did not necessarily 237 

capture the precise energy rates as in the validation studies, this was expected given the difficulty in reproducing 238 

experimental protocols as well as possible errors in the absolute heat measurements owing to challenges in 239 

calibration procedures (Woledge et al., 1985) and normalisation methods. We illustrated the performance of the 240 

mechano-bioenergetics model across a range of varied muscle fibre-types, contractile conditions, and stimulation 241 

intensities. 242 

Energetic behaviour varies substantially across muscle fibre-types (Barclay, 1996), and capturing these differ-243 

ences is important, as fibre-types, which vary within muscles, individuals, species, and across the lifespan (Dick 244 

et al., 2026; Queeno et al., 2023), are closely related to muscle function (Goldspink, 1977). Across fibre-types, 245 

the model reproduced lower initial efficiencies in fast-type fibres compared to slow-type fibres (Barclay & We-246 

ber, 2004), which is expected given higher energetic demands of cross-bridge cycling in fast-type fibres (Barclay, 247 

2019). While Barclay and Weber (2004) did not detect a difference in total efficiencies, we observed lower total 248 

efficiencies in the EDL - possibly a consequence of the same recovery parameters across fibres types. However, 249 

smaller changes in the total efficiency compared to initial efficiency across fibre-types were observed. The recovery 250 

time-constants were consistent across fibre-types, which is in agreement with Barclay and Weber (2004). The 251 

predicted recovery heat rates were comparable to experimental values, but likely overestimated as R > 1 in many 252 

simulations (Table 5); given the consistency of this overestimation, a scaling of rrec to obtain an R ≈ 1 could be 253 

made. The model demonstrated similar magnitudes of energetic rates between the fibre-types across contraction 254 

protocols (high shortening rate, low stimulation time for EDL and slow shortening rate, high stimulation time 255 

for soleus), which is consistent with experimental observations (Lewis & Barclay, 2014). Through capturing the 256 

the differences in fibre-type properties, we provide a model capable of making in vivo energy cost predictions, 257 

where fibre-type substantially influences energy use across a range of locomotor tasks (Swinnen et al., 2024). 258 

Muscle contractions in vivo are often cyclical during locomotor tasks with contraction frequencies dependent 259 

on the speed of locomotion (Alexander, 1983). While obtaining muscle-level energetic rates in vivo is critical 260 

for accurately predicting energetics (Lichtwark et al., 2025), existing mathematical models (e.g., Lichtwark and 261 

Wilson (2005) and Umberger et al. (2003)) do not account for the time-dependent behaviour of muscle contraction 262 

and few models capture the recovery processes (Konno et al., 2025; Tsianos & MacFadden, 2016). These factors 263 

are particularly important for predicting the energetics of non-steady locomotion where the number, frequency, 264 

and intensity of the contractions vary. As observed in Fig. 3C,D, following a series of 10 contractions at slow 265 

frequencies, most of the recovery process takes place during the contractions, while at faster frequencies the 266 

recovery process occurs following the contraction. Thus, not accounting for recovery processes neglects these 267 

energetic rates completely, and without the time-dependent components of the energetics model its not possible 268 

to capture the dependence on contraction frequency. 269 

This model was designed to be implemented at both the whole muscle level and, through validation of the 270 

model behaviour at low stimulation frequencies, the motor unit level. While we have informed the recovery model 271 

based on data from the mouse soleus (Barclay et al., 1995), the model would benefit from data on EDL recovery 272 

processes (to obtain fast-type muscle fibre properties) along with muscle energetic rates in the moderate frequency 273 

range of 5 Hz to 40 Hz (typical of in vivo motor unit firing rates (Enoka, 1995)). Here, we capture decreases in 274 

Ca 2+ transport heat with decreases in simulation frequency, which is important for modelling the variable in vivo 275 

firing rates and is not captured with existing models. For example, with the increase in stimulation frequency from 276 

2Hz to 256 Hz in the soleus we saw a decrease in energy from 96% to 39% of the second twitch with this model 277 

(Fig. 5C), while the Konno et al. (2025) model predicted an increase in energetic rates (Supplementary Material 278 

Figure S2) - a consequence of only using information on the activation of the muscle and not the Ca 2+ flow. 279 

While the model parameters presented here were informed by mouse data, the parameters in the bioenergetics 280
