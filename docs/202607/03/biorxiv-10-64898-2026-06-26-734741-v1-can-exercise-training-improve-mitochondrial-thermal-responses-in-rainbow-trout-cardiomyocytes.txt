1. Abstract 22 

Climate-driven warming is challenging the physiological limits of aquatic ectotherms, with 23 

cardiac performance emerging as one of the key determinants of thermal tolerance. Cardiac 24 

function relies on mitochondrial ATP production, and mitochondrial dysfunction has been 25 

linked to cardiac failure at critical temperatures. However, mitochondria are plastic and may 26 

represent a target for interventions aimed at improving thermal tolerance in fish. Exercise 27 

training improves whole-animal performance in fish, including cardiac thermal performance, 28 

and improves mitochondrial function in other taxa. However, its effects on the thermal 29 

sensitivity of cardiac mitochondria remain unknown. This study investigated whether 30 

exercise-training alters cardiac mitochondrial performance at optimal and critical 31 

temperatures in rainbow trout Oncorhynchus mykiss . Farmed rainbow trout were subjected to 32 

a four-week exercise training regime, while control fish remained under standard rearing 33 

conditions. Cardiac mitochondrial respiration was assessed in permeabilised heart fibres at 34 

16°C (optimal growth temperature) and 26°C (temperature associated with cardiac 35 

arrhythmia) and several biochemical and nuclear indicators were measured. No significant 36 

differences were detected between treatments for any measured variable. However, trained 37 

fish generally exhibited higher maximal respiratory capacities and respiratory control ratios, 38 

particularly at the elevated temperature, suggesting subtle improvements in mitochondrial 39 

function despite considerable inter-individual variation. Temperature influenced 40 

mitochondrial performance, increasing proton leak and reducing coupling efficiency. These 41 

findings demonstrate that cardiac mitochondrial function is thermally sensitive and represents 42 

a potential targeted for improving thermal resilience in aquaculture species. 43 

44 

2. Summary Statement 45 

Exercise training did not significantly alter cardiac mitochondrial function in rainbow trout 46 

but small improvements were observed under heat stress, suggesting modest improvements in 47 

mitochondrial function despite considerable inter-individual variation. 48

3. Introduction 50 

Anthropogenic climate change is causing significant negative impacts to aquatic 51 

environments. For aquatic ectotherms, warming waters dictate nearly all physiological 52 

processes and some species are now experiencing temperatures that surpass their thermal 53 

limits compromising health and increasing susceptibility to morbidity and mortality (Rummer 54 

et al., 2014). The teleost heart is extremely sensitive to heat stress, where changes in 55

temperature can limit cardiac output, a key determinate of a fish’s thermal tolerance (Eliason 56 

and Anttila, 2017). When temperatures exceed an organism’s sustainable temperature zone, 57 

several molecular mechanisms are impacted, implicating cellular and organ functions, and 58 

can subsequently lead to organism death (Ekström et al., 2025; Ern et al., 2023). 59 

60 

Cardiac functions are highly dependent on sustained aerobic ATP production through 61 

mitochondrial oxidative phosphorylation (Brieske et al., 2025; Long et al., 2022) and 62 

therefore mitochondrial dysfunction at elevated temperatures is a concern for cardiac 63 

function. Elevated temperatures are particularly detrimental to complex I performance, 64 

causing declines in mitochondrial oxidative phosphorylation efficiency mainly because of 65 

increased proton leak (Gerber et al., 2020; Hilton et al., 2010; Iftikar and Hickey, 2013; 66 

Iftikar et al., 2015; Leo et al., 2017; Michaelsen et al., 2021; Pichaud et al., 2017). Declines in 67 

mitochondrial oxidative phosphorylation efficiency at elevated temperatures corresponds 68 

with temperatures that cause cardiac arrhythmia and is thought to be one of the key 69 

contributors to heat failure of heat-stressed fish (Hilton et al., 2010; Iftikar and Hickey, 2013; 70 

Iftikar et al., 2015). 71 

72 

Mitochondria are, however, a highly plastic organelle and by manipulating mitochondrial 73 

performance could provide an alternative avenue to improving organism performance. 74 

Indeed, strategies enhancing mitochondrial performance (termed mitochondrial therapies) are 75 

emerging in various industries to improve whole organism performance and health. For 76 

example, breeding, exercise training, and nutrition are all proven strategies used as 77 

mitochondrial therapies appearing in medicine (Bishop et al., 2014; Conley, 2016; Coutinho 78 

et al., 2017; Faria et al., 2023; Hsu et al., 2022; Kumar et al., 2024; Yu et al., 2023), as well 79 

as livestock (Algothmi et al., 2024; Brajkovic et al., 2023; García-Roche et al., 2022; 80 

Lapointe, 2014; Peng et al., 2021; Sanglard et al., 2023; Slaska et al., 2016; Srirattana and St 81 

John, 2017; Toyomizu et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2015) and equine (Algothmi et al., 2024; 82 

García-Roche et al., 2022; Lapointe, 2014; Peng et al., 2021) production. The role of 83 

mitochondrial therapies in aquatic production, such as finfish farming, remains elusive, 84 

despite these interventions being adaptable to commercial practices. 85 

86 

Exercise training could present as a promising tool for enhancing mitochondrial function in 87 

fish given moderate exercise has led to improvements in fish performance at the whole-88 

organism level (Davison and Herbert, 2013; Espírito-Santo et al., 2026; McKenzie et al., 89

improve mitochondrial function (Bishop et al., 2014; Broome et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2021; 91 

Farhat et al., 2020; Jacobs et al., 2013; Zoladz et al., 2017). Exercise training with 92 

domesticated fish stocks enables multiple benefits and cross-tolerance abilities (Rodgers and 93 

Gomez Isaza, 2023b; Rodgers and Gomez Isaza, 2024). Exercise training enhances growth 94 

(Castro et al., 2011; Davison and Herbert, 2013; Palstra and Planas, 2011; Ytrestoyl et al., 95 

2020), feed efficiency (Prescott et al., 2024b), athleticism (Prescott et al., 2023), disease 96 

resistance (Castro et al., 2011; Castro et al., 2013; Tierney and Farrell, 2004), immunity (Liu 97

1990; Farrell et al., 1991; Nilsen et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2016), as well as thermal (Anttila 99 

et al., 2014; Pettinau et al., 2022) and hypoxia tolerance (Fu et al., 2011). However, very few 100 

studies have attributed these exercise-enhanced phenotypes to the underlying cellular 101 

changes, such as enhanced mitochondrial function (Chen et al., 2021; Farhat et al., 2020), and 102 

as far as we know, no study has yet investigated how training could affect the thermal 103 

sensitivity of mitochondria. Exercise training could influence mitochondrial dynamics 104 

(fission-fusion), biogenesis, oxidative stress resistance, and/or ATP synthesis, all of which 105 

could enhance mitochondrial ATP production. 106 

107 

Equipping fish with the physiological abilities to maintain function in the face of warming 108 

waters is essential to the future and success of aquaculture and conservation restocking 109 

programs. Globally, the aquaculture industry, particularly companies farming in nearshore 110 

sea pens, shallow ponds, and/or tanks with minimal temperature control are significantly 111 

impacted by the effects of warming waters and marine heatwaves. Heat-stress mortality 112 

events are frequently reported by farmers during summer months (Broekhuizen et al., 2021; 113 

Howarth et al., 2025; IPCC, 2022), and new adaptive measures are needed to safeguard fish 114 

welfare and industry sustainability. This study evaluates exercise training as a tool to 115 

modulate mitochondrial fitness and determines if exercise-enhanced mitochondrial fitness 116 

improves mitochondrial thermal performance and thereby lends support to whole-organism 117 

thermal tolerance. By combining in situ functional measurements with molecular tools for 118 

abundance estimates, our study provides insight into one possible strategy to shape 119 

mitochondrial fitness in finfish. 120

Animal husbandry 128 

The experimental procedures were conducted at the hatchery of the Natural Resources 129 

Institute Finland (Luke, Enonkoski, Finland) during summer 2024. Rainbow trout were 130 

initially reared in 130,000 L circular tanks and were moved to experimental tanks (1,650 L) 131 

on the 27 th May 2024 where they were kept under ambient temperature, oxygen, and light 132 

conditions until experiment started. Temperature and dissolved oxygen were measured daily. 133 

Water flow within the rearing tanks were maintained around 10 cm s -1 . The fish were fed ad 134 

libitum with a commercial feed optimised for rainbow trout (Ova 7S, Alltech Fennoaqua, 135 

Raisio, Finland). 136 

137 

From the 29th – 31 st July, fish were randomly distributed across six experimental tanks 138 

(1,000 L); three control tanks and three exercise treatment tanks. To minimise handling 139 

stress, three fish were sacrificed to obtain an approximate fish size; fork length was 40.3 ± 140 

0.07 cm and weight was 955 ± 1.25 g (means ± s.e.m.). The exercise treatment tanks were 141 

fitted with two pumps (Neptun NCTP-O 5000, 60W, 5000 L h -1 , Bauhaus, Mannheim, 142 

Germany) on the inside wall next to the water inlet. The two pumps were turned on and the 143 

incoming water was fully opened for six h each weekday to generate higher flows for 144 

exercising the fish. The average flow during these periods was 40 ± 0.36 cm s -1 and 10 ± 0.19 145 

cm s -1 for the exercise treatment and control, respectively. The exercise tanks during periods 146 

of rest exhibited flow speeds equal to the control group. Water speeds were measured twice 147 

per week for the entire four weeks of exercise training. 148

Mitochondrial respiration 150

temperatures were measured on cardiac tissue from thirteen control and fifteen exercise 152 

trained fish. The two temperatures were 16°C and 23°C, and were chosen as they correspond 153 

to the optimal growth temperature (Jobling, 1981) for rainbow trout and temperatures that 154 

induce cardiac arrhythmia (unpublished data from this study; Pettinau et al., 2022). A control 155 

and exercise trained fish were netted out of the experimental tank and were killed with a blunt 156 

cranial blow. The fish’s fork length and weight were measured and recorded, and the heart 157

Statistical Analysis 300 

All statistical analyses were performed using the R statistical language and lme4, glmmTMB, 301 

and MuMin packages. Data handling and figures were produced using tidyverse and ggplot2 302 

packages. Model selection was assessed using Akaike’s information criterion (AIC), 303 

following selection criteria from (Richards, 2005) Model parameters (normal distribution and 304 

equal variances) were assessed using DHARMa package. Non-parametric models were used 305 

for data that failed to meet model parameters. 306 

Normalisation of mitochondrial respiration rates was assessed in three different ways (per 307 

tissue mass, citrate synthase activity, and mtDNA:nDNA ratio), where per tissue mass was 308 

most appropriate and used for statistical analysis. For all mitochondrial respiration models, 309 

treatment and temperature were included as fixed factors and interactions between predicator 310 

and fixed variables were investigated. Repeated measures were included as a random effect 311 

nested within fish identification to account for independent responses. Fish weight, heart lipid 312 

content, and relative ventricle mass were included as co-variates and removed if not 313 

significant. Tank identification was included as a random factor, but was removed from all 314 

models during model comparisons. 315

6. Discussion 340 

Cardiac mitochondrial oxidative phosphorylation is a key contributor to cardiac dysfunction 341 

during thermal stress and therefore presents a focal point for better understanding, predicting, 342 

and enhancing organism thermal tolerance. For finfish farmers, applying this understanding 343 

of how mitochondrial function underpins organism performance and stressor tolerance could 344 

enable identification of new strategies to improve mitochondrial function and stock 345 

performance. We examined how exercise training alters cardiac mitochondrial function in 346 

rainbow trout at temperatures associated with optimal growth and cardiac arrhythmia. 347 

Exercise training resulted in modest changes in mitochondrial function, with some effects 348 

like leak, CI-CII-OXPHOS, and maximum respiration rates, becoming more apparent at 349 

critical temperatures. Furthermore, trained fish appeared to be more responsive to 350 

temperature at the higher temperature, although considerable inter-individual variation was 351 

observed. Temperature strongly influenced mitochondrial performance, with complexes I and 352 

II appearing particularly sensitive, suggesting a limitation for ATP production under thermal 353 

stress. 354 

355 

Exercise-enhanced mitochondrial function 356 

Exercise training in mammalian species improves mitochondrial ATP production through 357 

modulating fusion and fission processes, such as changes in mitochondrial abundance, 358 

morphology, and efficiency (Djalalvandi and Scorrano, 2022; Li et al., 2025). In the present 359 

study, exercise training induced modest, non-significant changes to mitochondrial dynamics, 360 

where small increases in mtDNA:nDNA and respiratory efficiencies were found and little 361 

change in citrate synthase activity, suggesting limited mitochondrial remodelling under these 362 

conditions. However, exercise-trained fish in general exhibit higher maximal respiratory 363 

capacity, involving complex I and II, with differences most evident at critical temperatures. 364 

This pattern, alongside elevated respiratory and leak control ratios at both temperatures 365 

indicates that exercise may enhance mitochondrial efficiency. However, absolute leak was 366 

only lower than control fish at optimal temperatures and were slightly higher under 367 

challenging temperatures. These mitochondrial responses are somewhat consistent with the 368 

small, non-significant increases in critical thermal maximum (CT max ) measured from a 369 

subsample of rainbow trout held under the same conditions in an analogous study and 370 

improvements in whole animal cardiac thermal tolerance observed in a previous study 371 

(Pettinau, 2023; Pettinau et al., 2022). Together, these small modifications suggest exercise 372

training can make modest contributions to mitochondrial function to improve thermal 373 

tolerance, but further exploration is needed. 374 

375 

Improved mitochondrial thermal responses driven by exercise training in rainbow trout may 376 

have been masked by substantial inter-individual variability within treatments. High 377 

variability was observed across mitochondrial respiration rates in both treatments, but 378 

particularly among trained fish when measured under elevated temperatures. This suggests 379 

that some individuals may respond more favourably to exercise-training than others, while 380 

for some fish the training regime itself may represent an additional physiological stressor or 381 

oppositely, not energy demanding enough. Contrasting responses to exercise-training may 382 

also reflect domestication pressures and genotype-by-environmental interactions (Prescott et 383 

al., 2024a). These individual responses are illustrated in Fig. S1, where some individuals 384 

(e.g., 8/13 control and 9/15 exercise trained individuals increased complexes I and II 385 

maximum mitochondrial respiration rates with elevated temperatures) exhibited increased 386 

respiration rates with elevated temperatures, whereas others showed marked declines. This 387 

pattern suggests that certain individuals were able to maintain mitochondrial function under 388 

challenging temperatures, while others exhibited signs of mitochondrial dysfunction. Future 389 

research should investigate whether individuals with divergent mitochondrial thermal 390 

responses differ in thermal tolerance and whether these traits are associated with 391 

responsiveness to exercise training. This would enable better understanding of the drivers of 392 

individual physiological variation and define reliable strategies for improving production 393 

stock’s thermal performance. 394 

395 

Although there were only some links evident between exercise training influencing 396 

mitochondrial ATP production, exercise training remains a promising strategy to enhance 397 

cardiac performance and thermal resilience in vulnerable stocks. Previous studies using 398 

similar training regimes have reported improvements in cardiovascular and thermal 399 

performance in rainbow trout, although these were conducted in smaller individuals 400 

(Papadopoulou et al., 2022; Pettinau et al., 2022). In the present study, cardiac energy content 401 

did not differ significantly between groups; although reductions in glucose and lipid levels in 402 

the exercise-trained fish suggest that the exercise-training regime imposed a greater energetic 403 

demand. This indicates that the training stimulus may have been sufficient to alter substrate 404 

utilisation, but insufficient to drive substantial mitochondrial remodelling. 405 

406

In other species, exercise training lowers the resting heart rate and overall energy demand 407 

during exercise (Hellsten and Nyberg, 2015). If fish also exhibit these cardiovascular changes 408 

with exercise training, it could also provide explanation why significant modulations in 409 

mitochondrial respiration rates weren’t observed and that slight changes in mitochondrial 410 

content was sufficient to meet the energy demands of increased energy expenditure. As such, 411 

more intensive exercise regimes requiring larger energy demands may have elicited greater 412 

changes in mitochondrial dynamics. Further optimisation in training intensity and duration 413 

may therefore be necessary to elicit more pronounced physiological and performance 414 

benefits, and the use of a varying flow regime could accommodate for individual variability. 415 

416 

Nonetheless, the modest changes in mitochondrial function observed provide some support 417 

for the altered performance observed in previous studies in exercise-trained fish (Anttila et 418 

al., 2014; Farrell et al., 1990; Palstra et al., 2015; Pettinau et al., 2022; Timmerhaus et al., 419 

2021), suggesting that even small shifts in mitochondrial performance may influence cardiac 420 

function under thermal stress (Pettinau et al., 2022). Beyond mitochondrial efficiency, 421 

additional cellular mechanisms may contribute to exercise-induced changes in thermal 422 

performance. For example, exercise training has been shown to modify the electrical 423 

excitability of cardiomyocytes in rainbow trout, including prolongation of the action potential 424 

(Le et al., 2026), which could influence cardiac stability at higher temperatures. Structural 425 

adaptations may also play a role; in mammals, exercise increases mitochondrial cristae 426 

density, enhancing oxidative phosphorylation capacity without requiring large increases in 427 

mitochondrial volume (Djalalvandi and Scorrano, 2022; Li et al., 2025; Nielsen et al., 2017). 428 

Furthermore, improved myocardial perfusion through increased capillary density and cardiac 429 

remodelling has been document in humans (Pinckard et al., 2019), potentially supporting 430 

oxygen delivery and metabolic demand during stress. Together, these mechanisms suggest 431 

that exercise-induced improvements in thermal performance may arise from integrated 432 

changes across multiple levels of cardiac organisation, rather than mitochondrial function 433 

alone. 434 

435 

Mitochondrial function thermal sensitivity 436 

Temperature had a strong influence on mitochondrial respiratory performance. Elevated 437 

temperatures increased leak respiration and the leak control ratio, indicating reduced 438 

mitochondrial membrane integrity and increased proton permeability, which together lowers 439 

OXPHOS efficiency. Although CI-linked respiration rates were higher at elevated 440

temperatures, this likely reflects accelerated metabolic flux rather than improved efficiency, 441 

as increased oxygen consumption was accompanied by greater uncoupling. A similar, albeit 442 

not significant, trend was observed for CII respiration. These responses are consistent with 443 

previous studies showing that elevated temperatures disproportionately impair complex I 444 

function and increase proton leak, ultimately reducing mitochondrial efficiency. This has 445 

been demonstrated in triplefin species (Family Tripterygiidae; Hilton et al., 2010), spotty 446 

wrasse Notolabrus celidotus (Iftikar and Hickey, 2013; Iftikar et al., 2015), rainbow trout 447 

Oncorhynchus mykiss (Michaelsen et al., 2021; Pichaud et al., 2017), Atlantic salmon Salmo 448 

salar (Gerber et al., 2020), Polar cod Boreogadus saida (Leo et al., 2017), and Atlantic cod 449 

Gadus morhua (Leo et al., 2017). The reduced mitochondrial ATP production efficiency 450 

observed here occurred at temperatures associated with cardiac arrhythmia in rainbow trout, 451 

supporting the idea that mitochondrial dysfunction contributes to cardiac failure under heat 452 

stress. Together these findings highlight a key trade-off at elevated temperatures, where 453 

increased respiratory rates are offset by reduced coupling efficiency, ultimately limiting ATP 454 

supply to the heart. 455 

456 

Conclusions and implications for industry 457 

Overall, our findings demonstrate that mitochondrial function plays a central role in shaping 458 

cardiac performance under thermal stress, with elevated temperatures driving increased 459 

respiratory demand but reduced coupling efficiency, ultimately limiting ATP supply. While 460 

exercise training elicited only modest improvements in mitochondrial performance, the 461 

enhanced responses observed under critical conditions suggest that mitochondrial function 462 

retains some plasticity and may be a viable target for intervention. In particular, the 463 

sensitivity of complex I-linked respiration to temperature highlights a potential bottleneck in 464 

cardiac energy metabolism during heat stress. For aquaculture, these findings suggest that 465 

strategies aimed at improving mitochondrial efficiency may enhance thermal resilience in 466 

farmed fish. However, the limited magnitude of responses observed here indicates that 467 

optimisation of such approaches will be necessary to achieve commercially relevant gains in 468 

performance. In particular, how large individual variance can drive population outcomes 469 

where some fish respond well to the training regime used and others did not. Perhaps, a 470 

varying flow regime that allows fish to select their swimming speed and thus effort could be a 471 

viable approach for industry. Future work should therefore focus on identifying interventions 472 

that more effectively target mitochondrial function and its integration with whole-organism 473 

physiology under warming conditions. 474
