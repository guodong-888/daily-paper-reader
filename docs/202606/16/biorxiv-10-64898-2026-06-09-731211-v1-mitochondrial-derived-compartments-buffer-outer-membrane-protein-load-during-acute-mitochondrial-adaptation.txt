Abstract 24 

Cells undergoing metabolic transitions rapidly remodel mitochondria through coordinated 25 

expansion and reorganization of the mitochondrial proteome. How the outer mitochondrial 26 

membrane (OMM) accommodates acute increases in newly synthesized proteins before 27 

organelle adaptation is complete remains poorly understood. Here we show that 28 

mitochondrial-derived compartments (MDCs), multilamellar domains that form from the 29 

OMM and selectively sequester OMM-associated cargo, arise during metabolic 30 

perturbations associated with acute mitochondrial biogenesis, including glucose 31 

restriction, carbon-source switching, and salt stress. In these situations, MDC formation 32 

requires the energy-sensing kinase Snf1 and derepression of the transcriptional repressor 33 

Mig1, linking MDC induction to transcriptional programs that increase mitochondrial 34 

protein expression. Activation of mitochondrial biogenesis in the absence of metabolic 35 

changes is sufficient to trigger MDCs, whereas disruption of mitochondrial protein 36 

targeting and import prevents MDC formation and causes mislocalization of outer 37 

membrane cargos. Together, these findings, combined with previous observations that 38 

MDCs are induced by hydrophobic protein overexpression, mistargeting, and metabolic 39 

perturbations, support an emerging model in which MDCs function as adaptive outer-40 

membrane remodeling domains that buffer outer membrane protein load during 41 

mitochondrial adaptation. 42

Introduction 47 

Cells frequently undergo metabolic transitions that require mitochondria to rapidly 48 

remodel their composition and function to meet new energetic demands (Nunnari & 49 

Suomalainen, 2012; Friedman & Nunnari, 2014; Wai, 2024). During these adaptations, 50 

mitochondria adjust their proteome, membrane composition, and metabolic capacity to 51 

support new functional states. Multiple mechanisms have been discovered that 52 

coordinate these changes. For example, mitochondrial dynamics reshape the network to 53 

match metabolic conditions (Westermann, 2012; Youle & Van Der Bliek, 2012; Wai, 54 

2024), mitophagy and vesicular trafficking pathways remove damaged or superfluous 55 

components (Palikaras et al , 2015; Pickles et al , 2018; Poillet-Perez & White, 2021), and 56 

transcriptional programs stimulate mitochondrial biogenesis to expand respiratory 57 

capacity (Kayikci & Nielsen, 2015; Piantadosi & Suliman, 2012; Martinez-Ortiz et al , 2019; 58 

Wang et al , 2024). In parallel, mitochondrial protein import and quality-control systems— 59 

including membrane potential–dependent import pathways and surveillance mechanisms 60 

that monitor mislocalized or unimported precursors—help maintain mitochondrial 61 

proteostasis during these transitions (Gebert et al , 2011; Kim et al , 2024; Wiedemann & 62 

Pfanner, 2017; Weidberg & Amon, 2018). Together, these processes remodel 63 

mitochondrial structure and composition as cells adapt to changing metabolic 64 

environments. 65 

66 

Despite these advances, acute metabolic transitions that are associated with increased 67 

mitochondrial biogenesis likely pose a fundamental challenge for mitochondria (Boos et 68 

al , 2019; Kim et al , 2024). These transitions often occur while mitochondrial function is 69

The mitochondrial-derived compartment (MDC) pathway is a recently described 79 

remodeling response of the mitochondrial outer membrane that may be uniquely 80 

positioned to buffer acute increases in OMM protein load. MDCs are multilamellar 81 

membrane domains derived from the OMM that selectively capture and sequester 82 

hydrophobic outer-membrane proteins away from the organelle (Hughes et al , 2016; 83 

Wilson et al , 2024b). Previous work has shown that MDC formation can be triggered by 84 

diverse metabolic perturbations that alter mitochondrial homeostasis, including changes 85 

in cellular amino acids, mitochondrial metabolites, and lipids (Schuler et al , 2021; 86 

Raghuram & Hughes, 2024; Xiao et al , 2024; Wong et al , 2025). MDCs can also form 87 

when hydrophobic proteins accumulate at mitochondria due to overexpression or 88 

mistargeting following failed insertion at another organelle (Wilson et al , 2024a). Recent 89 

work has further implicated Yme1-dependent proteolytic remodeling of mitochondrial 90 

lipid-transfer and membrane-architecture factors in enabling MDC biogenesis, supporting 91 

the idea that MDC formation requires coordinated remodeling of mitochondrial membrane 92

Results 118 

Physiological stresses that stimulate acute mitochondrial biogenesis induce MDC 119 

formation 120 

Previous work from our laboratory showed that MDC formation can be triggered by 121 

metabolic stresses that perturb mitochondrial homeostasis, including changes in amino-122 

acid availability or mitochondrial lipid balance, as well as by conditions that increase 123 

mitochondrial membrane protein load, such as forced overexpression or mistargeting of 124 

hydrophobic outer-membrane proteins (Hughes et al , 2016; Wilson et al , 2024a; Schuler 125 

et al , 2021; Raghuram & Hughes, 2024; Xiao et al , 2024; Wong et al , 2025). These 126 

observations suggested that MDCs arise when mitochondrial metabolic perturbation 127 

coincides with increased membrane protein burden. We therefore reasoned that acute 128 

metabolic transitions that alter mitochondrial metabolic state while also activating 129 

mitochondrial biogenesis might naturally generate these conditions. To test this idea, we 130 

examined metabolic stresses and transitions known to stimulate mitochondrial biogenesis 131 

and alter mitochondrial metabolism, including glucose restriction, abrupt carbon-source 132 

switching from fermentable to non-fermentable sugars, glycolytic inhibition, and salt 133 

stress (Coccetti et al, 2018 ; Martinez-Ortiz et al , 2019; Pastor et al , 2009; Posas et al ,134 

2000; Kayikci & Nielsen, 2015). 135

We first tested whether glucose restriction, a physiological inducer of mitochondrial 137 

biogenesis, triggers MDC formation. MDCs were monitored by fluorescence microscopy 138 

as previously described (Schuler et al , 2021) using the outer-membrane marker Tom70-139 

GFP together with the inner-membrane marker Tim50-mCherry, where MDCs appear as 140 

Tom70-positive domains that exclude Tim50 signal. Consistent with activation of 141 

mitochondrial biogenesis, several mitochondrial proteins—including the outer-membrane 142 

markers Om45 and Por1 as well as the matrix enzyme Cit1—displayed increased 143 

expression within one to two hours after glucose restriction (Fig. 1A, Fig. S1A). Strikingly, 144 

shifting cells from rich medium containing 2% glucose to rich medium without added 145 

glucose induced robust MDC formation, with 70–80% of cells forming Tom70-enriched 146 

MDCs within 2 hours (Fig. 1B-C). 147 

148 

Glucose restriction–induced MDCs appeared rapidly and excluded the inner-membrane 149 

marker Tim50-mCherry. Time-lapse imaging revealed that MDC biogenesis began with 150 

the formation of Tom70-GFP foci at mitochondria, which subsequently elongated and 151 

matured into multilamellar spherical structures within 0.5-1 h of treatment (Movie S1). 152 

Mature MDCs generated during glucose restriction reached approximately 0.70 microns 153 

in diameter on average, similar to previously reported MDCs induced by the mTOR 154 

inhibitor rapamycin (Fig. S1B). The cargo profile of glucose-restriction induced MDCs also 155 

resembled that of previously characterized MDCs triggered by treatment with rapamycin 156 

(Fig. S1C-D). In addition, glucose restriction–induced MDC cargos were delivered to the 157 

vacuole and degraded through the same autophagy- and vacuolar protease-dependent 158 

pathway used by MDCs formed under other stresses. GFP-processing assays showed 159

We next asked whether other perturbations of glycolytic metabolism similarly induce MDC 167 

formation. Shifting cells from glucose to alternative carbon sources produced variable 168 

levels of MDC induction (Fig. 1E). These differences largely correlated with the degree of 169 

metabolic reprogramming required: switching to carbon sources that remain primarily 170 

fermentative, such as fructose, failed to induce MDCs, whereas carbon sources that are 171 

non-fermentable or require transcriptional activation of alternative metabolic programs, 172 

such as galactose through the GAL network, produced strong MDC responses (Nehlin et 173 

al , 1991; Platt & Reece, 1998). Inhibition of glycolysis with the starvation mimic 2-deoxy-174 

D-glucose (2-DG) also induced MDC formation at intermediate concentrations, while 175 

higher concentrations failed to induce MDCs (Fig. S1F). These findings suggest that MDC 176 

biogenesis is activated when glycolytic metabolism is altered. 177

MDC formation during glucose restriction requires trace glucose but is 195 

independent of amino-acid availability 196 

To better understand how glucose restriction induces MDC formation, we next 197 

investigated the metabolic requirements underlying this response. Our previous work 198 

showed that several MDC-inducing stresses—including mTOR inhibition, impaired 199 

vacuolar function, and translation perturbation—stimulated MDC formation by elevating 200 

intracellular amino acid pools that led to downstream amino-acid–dependent alterations 201 

in mitochondrial metabolism (Schuler et al , 2021; Raghuram & Hughes, 2024). Because 202 

glucose restriction slows translation and increases intracellular amino-acid pools, we 203 

initially hypothesized that MDC formation during glucose restriction might similarly arise 204 

from changes in amino-acid levels (Ashe et al , 2000; Schuler et al , 2021). 205

Consistent with this possibility, glucose restriction or carbon switching in synthetic 207 

medium, which contains substantially lower amino-acid concentrations than rich YPAD 208 

medium, failed to induce MDCs (Fig. 2A and Fig. S2A). Instead, mitochondria became 209 

highly fragmented (Fig. 2B). These observations initially suggested that amino-acid 210 

limitation might suppress MDC formation under these conditions. If this were the case, 211 

restoring amino-acid availability should rescue MDC formation. However, unlike 212 

previously reported MDC-inducing stresses such as rapamycin, concanamycin A213 

(ConcA), and cycloheximide (CHX), addition of purified amino-acid sources such as 214 

casein or peptone failed to restore MDC formation. Only addition of yeast extract—which 215 

contains lipids, peptides, and metabolizable carbohydrates in addition to amino acids— 216 

readily restored MDC formation (Fig. 2C). 217 

218 

Because purified amino-acid sources failed to rescue this response, but yeast extract did, 219 

we considered whether a non-amino-acid component of yeast extract, such as residual 220 

metabolizable carbon, was important. Previous studies have shown that synthetic 221 

medium with added glucose contains little to no residual glucose, whereas rich medium 222 

retains small amounts of metabolizable carbon after glucose removal due to components 223 

of yeast extract (Adachi et al , 2017; Ashe et al , 2000; Weber et al , 2020). We therefore 224 

asked whether it was the complete absence of glucose that was suppressing MDC 225 

formation in synthetic media. Consistent with this idea, adding trace amounts of glucose 226 

(0.02%) into glucose-free synthetic medium was sufficient to restore MDC formation and 227 

mitochondrial structure in this media type, producing MDC levels comparable to those 228

Consistent with the requirement for the presence of trace glucose for MDCs to form, MDC 234

Together, these results show that glucose-restriction MDC formation requires reduced, 278 

but not absent, glucose availability and occurs under metabolic states that preserve 279 

aspects of central carbon metabolism. Unlike previously characterized MDC-inducing 280 

conditions such as concanamycin A, rapamycin, and cycloheximide, this response is 281

Snf1 is required for glucose restriction- and salt stress-induced MDCs 286 

To understand what drives MDC formation during glucose-dependent metabolic 287 

transitions, we next examined signaling pathways that couple changes in carbon 288 

availability to MDC induction. Because glucose restriction and carbon-source switching 289 

engage well-defined glucose-responsive regulatory networks, we focused on major 290 

carbon-responsive pathways (Kunkel et al , 2019; Kayikci & Nielsen, 2015). Among the 291 

major glucose-sensing pathways, deletion of SNF1 , a central energy-stress kinase that 292 

promotes respiratory metabolism, completely blocked MDC formation induced by glucose 293 

restriction and carbon-source switching, whereas deletion of the PKA-pathway regulators 294 

IRA2 , GPA2 , or GPR1 had no effect (Fig. 3A-B; Fig. S3A). Because Snf1 activates 295 

mitochondrial biogenesis through transcriptional derepression, we next tested additional 296 

regulators of this pathway (Kayikci & Nielsen, 2015; Coccetti et al , 2018; Martinez-Ortiz 297

Transcriptional derepression via Mig1 is required downstream of Snf1 in MDC 328 

induction 329 

To identify the downstream effector(s) of Snf1 that drive MDC formation during glucose-330 

dependent metabolic transitions, we examined Snf1-regulated pathways known to 331 

influence mitochondrial metabolism and remodeling. Snf1 has established roles in the 332 

regulation of autophagy, mitophagy, β-oxidation, lipid metabolism, and transcriptional 333 

control of glucose derepression (Usaite et al , 2009; Kayikci & Nielsen, 2015; Wei et al ,334 

2016; Yao et al , 2020). Among these, only perturbation of the glucose derepression 335 

program consistently altered MDC induction during glucose restriction (Fig. 4A–C; Fig. 336 

S4A-B). Specifically, disruption of pathways downstream of Snf1 involved in β-oxidation 337 

(FOX2 ), stress and nutrient signaling (RIM15 ), autophagy (ATG11 ), or mitophagy 338 

(ATG32 ) had no detectable effect on glucose restriction–induced MDC formation (Fig. 339 

S4A). Likewise, perturbation of Snf1-regulated lipid metabolism did not phenocopy the 340 

specific requirement for Snf1 in glucose-responsive MDC induction. Acute repression of 341 

ACC1 , a rate-limiting enzyme in de novo fatty acid synthesis and a well-established Snf1 342 

target during glucose restriction (Wei et al , 2016), did not stimulate MDC formation and 343

Under glucose-rich conditions, the transcriptional repressor Mig1 contributes to 352 

repression of glucose-derepressed genes, including genes involved in alternative carbon 353 

metabolism and respiratory growth. Upon glucose limitation, Snf1 phosphorylates Mig1, 354 

promoting transcriptional derepression (Fig. 4A) (Treitel et al , 1998; Kayikci & Nielsen, 355 

2015). Consistent with this model, deletion of MIG1 fully restored MDC formation in snf1Δ 356 

cells under both glucose restriction and salt stress (Fig. 4B–D), demonstrating that relief 357 

of Mig1-mediated repression is sufficient to bypass the requirement for Snf1. Additionally, 358 

Snf1-dependent phosphorylation sites on Mig1 have been shown to be required for proper 359 

transcriptional derepression (Treitel et al , 1998). Consistent with this, CRISPR-generated 360 

Mig1 phospho-null mutants reduced glucose restriction-induced MDC formation, albeit to 361 

varying degrees (Fig. S4F). 362

prevented increased expression of mitochondrial proteins during glucose restriction and 365

Discussion 459 

Metabolic transitions require cells to rapidly increase mitochondrial protein synthesis and 460 

remodel organelle composition to meet changing energetic demands (Pfanner et al , 2019; 461 

Palikaras et al , 2015). While the transcriptional programs that drive mitochondrial 462 

biogenesis are well established, how cells maintain mitochondrial proteostasis during 463 

these acute transitions has remained unclear (Kayikci & Nielsen, 2015; Posas et al , 2000; 464 

Boos et al , 2019; Kim et al , 2024). Here, we show that mitochondrial-derived 465 

compartments (MDCs), previously identified multilamellar structures derived from the 466 

outer mitochondrial membrane, are engaged during acute mitochondrial adaptation. 467 

Glucose restriction, carbon-source switching, and osmotic stress each induce a Snf1-468 

dependent transcriptional program that increases mitochondrial protein expression, and 469 

under these conditions MDCs form robustly. MDC formation requires ongoing 470 

transcription and Tom70/Tom71-dependent mitochondrial protein targeting but remains 471 

modest when mitochondrial biogenesis is induced in the absence of metabolic 472 

perturbation and is suppressed after prolonged adaptation. Together, these findings 473 

support a model in which MDCs are most strongly engaged when increased mitochondrial 474 

protein influx converges with a mitochondrial state undergoing acute metabolic 475 

remodeling. 476

Based on these findings, we propose that MDCs may function as transient outer-478 

membrane buffering domains that sequester membrane proteins delivered during periods 479 

when protein influx may exceed the immediate capacity of mitochondrial membrane 480 

organization and assembly pathways . Consistent with this model, disruption of 481 

mitochondrial protein targeting causes mitochondrial proteins to mislocalize and prevents 482 

MDC formation despite continued induction of mitochondrial protein expression. Thus, 483 

MDCs are not simply a consequence of increased protein synthesis, but require delivery 484 

and engagement of newly synthesized proteins with the mitochondrial outer membrane 485 

(Xiao et al , 2024, 2021). By concentrating these proteins within a discrete OMM-derived 486 

domain, MDCs may temporarily limit their accumulation across the broader mitochondrial 487 

network until the organelle adapts or the cargos are delivered to the vacuole for 488 

degradation (Wilson et al , 2024a). 489 

490 

This framework provides a possible unifying interpretation for prior MDC-inducing 491 

conditions (Fig. 6H). Many previously described triggers—including pharmacological 492 

inhibition of mTOR, V-ATPase disruption, translational stress, direct mitochondrial 493 

membrane protein overexpression, alterations in membrane lipid composition, and 494 

protein mistargeting—either increase hydrophobic membrane protein burden at the 495 

organelle, alter mitochondrial metabolic state, or both (Schuler et al , 2021; Raghuram & 496 

Hughes, 2024; Xiao et al , 2024; Wong et al , 2025; Wilson et al , 2024a). Thus, MDC 497 

formation appears to be favored when two conditions converge: elevated hydrophobic 498 

protein burden at the OMM and a mitochondrial state undergoing acute remodeling or 499 

adaptation. Consistent with this model, acute induction of mitochondrial biogenesis was 500

This interpretation is further supported by prior rapamycin-based genetic screening, which 509

Importantly, our study establishes MDCs as a physiologically relevant response 517 

associated with acute mitochondrial biogenesis rather than a phenomenon restricted to 518 

pharmacological perturbations. Under glucose restriction and related metabolic 519 

transitions, MDCs form during endogenous carbon-responsive remodeling programs. 520 

This places MDC formation early in the adaptive response, before cells resort to broader 521 

mitochondrial turnover pathways such as mitophagy (Palikaras et al , 2015; Kayikci & 522

Several key questions remain. First, the molecular trigger for MDC formation is unknown. 527 

Although our data support a role for increased outer-membrane protein load, how this 528 

state is sensed and converted into membrane remodeling remains unclear. Second, the 529 

machinery that drives MDC formation has not been fully elucidated. Whether MDCs arise 530 

through regulated membrane deformation, lipid redistribution, or dedicated scission 531 

mechanisms remains an important question. Third, the functional consequences of MDC 532 

formation during mitochondrial biogenesis remain difficult to assess. Current approaches 533 

to block MDC formation disrupt upstream processes such as protein import or membrane 534 

organization, making it challenging to isolate the specific contribution of MDCs to 535 

mitochondrial homeostasis or directly test whether they buffer acute protein load. 536 

537 

In summary, we propose that MDCs are adaptive outer-membrane remodeling domains 538 

that respond to hydrophobic membrane protein load during acute mitochondrial 539 

adaptation. Rather than being triggered by protein load or metabolic stress alone, MDC 540 

formation is most strongly engaged when elevated outer-membrane protein burden 541 

coincides with a mitochondrial state undergoing active remodeling. By linking 542 

mitochondrial protein influx to membrane remodeling, this work positions MDCs as a 543 

physiologically relevant proteostasis pathway that may sequester and manage excess 544 

outer-membrane proteins while mitochondrial adaptation is underway. 545

Isolation of yeast mitochondria was done as described in (Balasubramaniam et al , 2026). 733
