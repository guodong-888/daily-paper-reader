Abstract 35 We constructed a spatially resolved metabolomic atlas of long-term exercise across six major 36 organs in wild-type mice: brain, heart, lung, liver, kidney, and skeletal muscle, cataloguing 224 37 metabolic features and revealing coordinated inter-organ remodeling. Surprisingly, the brain 38 showed particularly pronounced region-specific adaptation. Because pathological tau associates 39 with synaptic mitochondria from early stages of tauopathy, we extended this multi-organ spatial 40 metabolomic approach to PS19 mice and found that exercise reduced over 70% of observable 41 tau pathology in PS19 hippocampus and restored the mitochondrial-related metabolome. 42 Integrated proteomic and spatial metabolomic analyses identified NADH dehydrogenase 43 Complex I as the convergent node. To test this finding biologically, we expressed the yeast 44 NADH dehydrogenase, Ndi1, in PS19 neurons in the absence of exercise. This increased 45 cerebral antioxidants, restored shuttle-linked metabolites, and reduced tau pathology. Increasing 46 NADH dehydrogenase activity through NDI1 reproduces the core anti-tau and metabolic effects 47 of exercise. These findings provide a molecular mechanism for how exercise may prevent or 48 slow tau pathology accumulation, complementing the human-cohort literature linking exercise to 49 delayed cognitive decline. 50 51 

Introduction 52 Regular physical exercise induces coordinated physiological adaptations across nearly every 53 major organ system and is among the top non-pharmacological interventions to combat non-54 communicable disease and promote healthy aging (1–7). Work over the past decade has 55 established that exercise can elicit systemic effects via circulating molecules termed ‘exerkines’ 56 (8), as well as exercise-inducible metabolites with systemic effects on feeding and energy 57 balance (9, 10). These ‘exerkines’ are secreted from muscle, liver, bone, and adipose, and are 58 responsible for many of the health benefits observed with exercise. 59 60 Among the organs influenced by exercise, the brain is particularly responsive. Regular physical 61 exercise protects cognitive function across the lifespan (1, 11–13). Voluntary wheel running in 62 rodents remodels the adult hippocampus, promoting neurogenesis, synaptic plasticity, and 63 spatial memory (11, 14). Endurance training in older adults increases hippocampal volume and 64 protects against age-related cognitive decline (12, 15). In humans at risk of Alzheimer’s disease 65 (AD), regular physical activity is associated with slower tau accumulation, lower neurofilament 66 light chain, and preserved cognition (16–18). Lifestyle trials including the U.S. POINTER study 67 now provide randomized evidence that structured exercise can meaningfully alter dementia risk 68

Single-cell and spatial omics studies in mouse models of brain aging and Alzheimer’s 69 disease have begun resolving the cellular and regional architecture of exercise responses in the 70 diseased brain (21, 22). Tau pathology is the principal neuropathological and imaging correlate 71 of synaptic loss and cognitive decline across AD and primary tauopathies (23). Tau is 72 predominantly a neuronal protein, and pathological tau aggregates initiate within neurons and 73 propagate trans-synaptically along connected circuits in AD (24, 25), locating the disease at the 74 level of neuronal cell biology. Yet despite consistent evidence that exercise prevents or slows 75 cognitive decline in at-risk populations, the molecular mechanisms through which exercise 76 engages the diseased brain, particularly in primary tauopathy, remain poorly defined (1, 26). 77 Defining these mechanisms in animal models that permit early intervention is essential for 78 developing pharmacologic strategies for patients unable to exercise. 79 80 In mouse tauopathy models, pathological tau associates with synaptic mitochondria as early as 81 3 months of age, coinciding with synaptic energetic dysfunction and excitatory synapse loss 82 (27). Mitochondrial respiration, biogenesis, and dynamics are similarly impaired well before 83 overt neurodegeneration (28, 29). Brain bioenergetic dysfunction is another modifier of 84 dementia. Hippocampal glucose hypometabolism precedes clinical cognitive decline by several 85 years on FDG-PET (30), and inherited and age-dependent bioenergetic decline is positioned 86 upstream of both A β and tau pathology in the mitochondrial cascade hypothesis (31, 32). This 87 places brain energy metabolism among the most actionable axes of dementia therapy (33). 88 Among bioenergetic defects in AD, the most consistent is electron transport chain impairment. 89 Decrements in Complex I and cytochrome c oxidase activity have been documented in AD 90 platelets and post-mortem cortex since the early 1990s (34, 35) and are featured prominently in 91 neuropathological surveys of mitochondrial dysfunction (36–38); more recently, in vivo PET 92 imaging has linked Complex I dysfunction to neurodegeneration and cognitive decline in AD 93 (39). Neurons are especially sensitive: synaptic transmission consumes a disproportionate 94 share of neuronal ATP, and sustained oxidative phosphorylation requires constant re-oxidation 95 of cytosolic NADH to fuel the electron transport chain via Complex I (40, 41). Complex I activity 96 is therefore crucial for normal brain function. Ndi1 thus provides a unique tool to test the phenotypic consequences of restoring 106 electron flow in biological systems. 107 108 Here, we leveraged matrix-assisted laser desorption/ionization mass spectrometry imaging 109 (MALDI-MSI) (48, 49) to generate a multi-organ spatial metabolomic atlas of the long-term 110 exercise response in wild-type mice after 3 months of voluntary wheel running. Together, these results 121 highlight the therapeutic potential of exercise mimetics and position neuronal Complex I as a 122 candidate bioenergetic node engaged by the exercise response. 123 124

While not all 15 metabolic 388 changes were directionally conserved with exercise, all MAS-associated metabolites increased 389 by exercise in PS19 were also increased by NDI1 (Fig. 5K). All PS19 brains across exercise and 390 NDI1 cohorts were pooled, and their brain-wide MAS module expression was scored. Just as for 391 exercise, NDI1 treatment restored MAS module scores near WT levels (Fig. 5 L and M). 392 Concordant rescue across all 3 endpoints – tau pathology, spatial metabolome, and brain 393 proteome – establishes augmenting neuronal Complex I activity as sufficient to reproduce the 394 multi-modal exercise-associated phenotype – tau pathology, spatial metabolome, and brain 395 proteome – in PS19 mice. 396 397 

Discussion 398 Collectively, our findings position neuronal Complex I as a convergent bioenergetic axis 399 associated with the exercise training response in primary tauopathy. Independent metabolomic 400 and proteomic signatures converged on the malate–aspartate shuttle and Complex I electron 401 flow. A neuron-targeted genetic bypass of Complex I, in the absence of exercise, reproduced the 402 core anti-tau and metabolic features of the response. Concordance of 3 orthogonal endpoints 403 (anti-tau pathology, spatial brain metabolome, and whole-brain proteome) in the same animals 404 distinguishes augmenting neuronal Complex I activity as sufficient to reproduce the anti-tau and 405 metabolic phenotype seen with exercise, rather than acting as a non-specific correlate.
